Path Following — Pure Pursuit

Give the robot a path and one delightfully simple rule: always chase a point a little way ahead.

Your autonomous has a plan: a curve across the field from the start to a scoring spot. The robot knows where it is (odometry, Lesson 15). How does it actually follow the path smoothly, instead of driving to each waypoint and stopping? The classic answer — used by FRC and FTC teams for years — is Pure Pursuit, and it's almost suspiciously simple.

🥕 Carrot on a stick. Imagine a carrot floating a fixed distance ahead of you, always on the path. You just keep walking toward the carrot. As you move, the carrot slides further along the path, so you're forever curving toward it — and you end up tracing the whole path smoothly. That floating carrot is the lookahead point.

The whole algorithm

  1. Find the lookahead point. Walk along the path from where you are until you're a fixed lookahead distance Ld away. That point is your carrot.
  2. Look at it from the robot's point of view. Convert the carrot into the robot's own frame, where +x is straight ahead and +y is to the left. Call the sideways offset y.
  3. Curve toward it. There's exactly one circular arc that starts at the robot (pointing forward) and passes through the carrot. Its curvature is:
curvature = 2 · y / Ld²   (y = how far left/right the carrot is)

turn rate ω = curvature · v   (v = your forward speed)

That little formula falls right out of geometry: if the carrot is dead ahead (y = 0), curvature is 0 — drive straight. The further off to the side it is, the harder you curve. Drive forward at v and turn at ω, and repeat every loop. That's the whole controller.

The one knob: lookahead distance

It's a balance, and feeling it is the best way to learn it:

Lookahead LdBehaviour
Too smallHugs the path tightly but gets twitchy and oscillates — overcorrects, weaves side to side.
Too largeButtery smooth but cuts corners and rounds off the path, missing tight features.
Just rightSmoothly tracks the path with gentle, stable correction.

Try this: drag two waypoints into a sharp dog-leg. Set lookahead small — the robot tracks the corner but jitters. Crank lookahead up — smooth as silk, but it slices straight across the corner. There's no "perfect" value; you pick based on how tight your path needs to be.
FTCFRC pure pursuit, one loop iteration
Pose2d robot = odometry.getPoseMeters();

// 1. Lookahead point: first path point at least Ld ahead (don't look backwards).
Translation2d carrot = path.get(path.size() - 1);          // default to the end
for (int i = lastIndex; i < path.size(); i++) {
    if (robot.getTranslation().getDistance(path.get(i)) >= LOOKAHEAD) {
        carrot = path.get(i);
        lastIndex = i;        // remember progress so we never target behind us
        break;
    }
}

// 2. Carrot in the robot's frame: +x forward, +y left.
Translation2d local = carrot.minus(robot.getTranslation())
                            .rotateBy(robot.getRotation().unaryMinus());

// 3. Arc curvature to the carrot, then command forward speed + turn rate.
double L         = local.getNorm();
double curvature = 2 * local.getY() / (L * L);
double omega     = curvature * CRUISE_SPEED;

drivetrain.drive(CRUISE_SPEED, omega);   // feed into the kinematics from Lesson 14
🚀
What the real libraries use. Pure pursuit is the gateway idea. Production followers go further: FRC's RamseteController and HolonomicDriveController, and FTC's Road Runner and Pedro Pathing, follow full trajectories (paths with a motion profile and timing) and add feedforward + PID on top. Same spirit — look ahead, curve toward the goal — with more accuracy and speed control.

Driving the whole robot around is one kind of "go to a point." The other is moving a jointed arm so its hand lands on a target — which needs the math to run backwards: inverse kinematics.
