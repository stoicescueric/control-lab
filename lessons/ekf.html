The Extended Kalman Filter

Kalman's worldly sibling — for when reality refuses to move in straight lines.

The plain Kalman filter has one big assumption baked in: that everything is linear — straight lines, simple proportions. But real sensors are often curvy. A radar measures distance and angle, not neat x-y coordinates. A thermometer's voltage bends with temperature. The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) is the patch that lets Kalman cope with these nonlinear situations — and it's probably the most widely deployed estimator on the planet (GPS, drones, robots).

What "nonlinear" means here

📡 Radar analogy. A radar dish doesn't see "the plane is at x=40, y=12." It sees "something is 30 km away, at a bearing of 22°." Turning distance-and-angle into x-y involves sine and cosine — curved math. And a tiny angle error becomes a huge position error when the target is far away. That's nonlinearity biting.

The one clever trick: zoom in until it's straight

Here's the whole idea of the EKF. A curve, if you zoom in close enough on any point, looks like a straight line (think of how the Earth feels flat even though it's round). So at every step the EKF:

  1. Looks at its current best guess.
  2. Pretends the curve is a straight line right there (this is called linearizing, using a bit of calculus called the Jacobian — just the local slope).
  3. Runs the normal Kalman math on that straight-line approximation.
  4. Repeats next step from the new guess.
🧮
In one sentence: the EKF = the Kalman filter + "linearize the curvy parts around my current estimate at every step." Same predict-update heartbeat, same Kalman gain — it just straightens the curve first.

See it: tracking a target with radar

The radar at the left only measures distance + angle to the target. Because of angle noise, the raw measurements smear out into curved arcs — and smear more the farther away the target is. The EKF still reconstructs a smooth, accurate estimate of the true path.

EKF vs. Kalman — when to reach for which

Kalman (KF)Extended Kalman (EKF)
World is…Linear (straight-line)Nonlinear (curves, angles, spin)
Extra workNoneCompute a slope (Jacobian) each step
AccuracyExact (for linear)Approximate — great if curves are gentle
Used inSimple tracking, financeGPS, drones, robot navigation, SLAM
⚠️
The EKF's straight-line trick only works if the curve is gentle near your guess. If things are wildly curvy or your guess is way off, the approximation can mislead it. That's why fancier cousins exist (the Unscented Kalman Filter, particle filters) — but the EKF remains the trusty workhorse.
