🧭 Which tool should I use?

A rough field guide. Real engineering mixes and matches, but this is a sane starting point.

Picking a filter (to clean up measurements)

If you…Reach for
Just need quick, cheap smoothing of one shaky valueLow-pass filter
Want to flatten steady random jitter and don't mind some lagMoving average
Have two sensors good in opposite ways (e.g. gyro + accelerometer)Complementary filter
Need the best possible estimate of something moving predictablyKalman filter
Same, but the sensor or motion is curvy/nonlinear (angles, GPS)Extended Kalman filter

Picking a controller (to decide actions)

If you…Reach for
Have an actuator that's only ON/OFF and a slow system (heater, fridge)Bang-bang
Need smooth, accurate control of most things (motors, drones, temperature)PID
Must respect hard limits, plan ahead, or juggle several controls at onceMPC

On an FTC/FRC robot, specifically

If you want to…Reach for
Hold a steady speed or angle accurately and snappilyFeedforward + PID
Move a slide/arm/robot smoothly to a spot (no slamming)Motion profile + feedforward
Turn a joystick into wheel commandsDrive kinematics
Know where the robot is on the fieldOdometry (corrected by vision)
Follow a planned route in autonomousPure pursuit / trajectory follower
Put a multi-joint arm's hand at a pointInverse kinematics

🧠 The big picture in one breath

🌟
A sensor gives you a noisy measurement. A filter turns it into a trustworthy estimate. A controller compares that estimate to your setpoint, computes the error, and chooses an action for the actuator. The world changes, the sensor reads again, and the whole feedback loop repeats — many times a second, forever. That's control theory.
