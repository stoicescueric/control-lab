Feedforward — predict, don't just react

The upgrade that turns a sluggish, twitchy PID loop into a crisp one. kS, kV, kA.

PID has a weakness: it's reactive. It does nothing until an error appears, then scrambles to fix it. But on a robot you often already know roughly what it'll take. Want the drivetrain at 3 m/s? You know about how much voltage that needs. Holding an arm out sideways? You know gravity is pulling on it. Feedforward is you handing the motor that known answer up front, so PID only has to clean up the small leftover.

🚗 Driving up a hill. A good driver presses the gas before the car slows down, because they can see the hill coming. A purely reactive driver waits until the car is already crawling, then stomps the pedal — lurchy and late. Feedforward is "see the hill, press the gas." PID is "notice you slowed down, correct it."

The formula that runs FRC drivetrains

The workhorse is WPILib's SimpleMotorFeedforward. To get a mechanism to a target velocity v (and optionally acceleration a), the voltage it predicts is:

V = kS·sign(v) + kV·v + kA·a

Three constants, each with a clear physical meaning:

TermFights…In words
kS — staticFriction / stiction The minimum voltage just to get it moving at all. A fixed shove in the right direction.
kV — velocityBack-EMF / drag Volts per unit of speed. The bulk of the work: "to go this fast, hold this voltage."
kA — accelerationInertia (mass) Extra volts to speed up this fast. Only matters while the target is changing.
🪜
Two close cousins you'll meet: an arm also fights gravity that changes with angle, so ArmFeedforward adds kG·cos(θ). An elevator fights constant gravity, so ElevatorFeedforward adds a plain kG. Same idea, one extra term for weight.

The golden pattern: feedforward + feedback

You almost never use feedforward alone (your constants are never perfect, and the world has surprises) or PID alone (laggy). You add them. Feedforward does ~90% of the work instantly; PID nudges the last bit and handles disturbances.

output = feedforward(goal) + PID(measurement, goal)
FRC · WPILib velocity control, the standard recipe
// Constants come from characterization (see below). Units: volts.
SimpleMotorFeedforward ff  = new SimpleMotorFeedforward(kS, kV, kA);
PIDController          pid = new PIDController(kP, 0, 0);

@Override
public void robotPeriodic() {
    double goalVel = 3.0;                        // m/s we want right now
    double measVel = encoder.getRate();          // m/s we actually have

    double predict = ff.calculate(goalVel);       // the "press the gas early" part
    double correct = pid.calculate(measVel, goalVel); // clean up the leftover error

    motor.setVoltage(predict + correct);          // volts, not -1..1
}
FTC the same thing, written out by hand
double predict = kS * Math.signum(goalVel) + kV * goalVel;  // (+ kA*goalAccel)
double correct = kP * (goalVel - measVel);
double volts   = predict + correct;

// FTC motors take power in -1..1, so divide by the real battery voltage:
motor.setPower(volts / battery.getVoltage());
🔋
Why divide by battery voltage? "0.5 power" means something different on a fresh 13 V battery than a tired 11 V one. Thinking in volts and dividing by the real pack voltage makes your robot behave the same all match long. WPILib's setVoltage() does this for you.

Three things to try:
  1. With FF ON and a tiny kP (say 2), it snaps to the target and sits there. The green FF bar is doing almost all the work.
  2. Turn FF OFF. Now PID alone with kP=2 settles well below the target — classic steady-state error. To fix it you'd need a big kP (try cranking it) which makes it twitchy and overshoot.
  3. Turn FF back on, then mistune kV too low. The FF under-predicts and PID has to make up the gap — you've lost the main benefit.

Where do kS, kV, kA come from?

You don't guess them blindly. Two honest ways:

  • Measure (best). FRC's SysId tool drives the mechanism through test patterns and computes kS, kV, kA for you. FTC folks do the same idea by hand or with library tuners (Road Runner's tuning OpModes).
  • Reason about it. kV ≈ 12 V ÷ (top speed) — if full voltage gives 4.8 m/s, then kV ≈ 12/4.8 = 2.5. kS = the voltage where it just barely starts creeping. kA is smaller and trickier; SysId really earns its keep here.
⚠️
Feedforward needs trustworthy units. kV in "volts per m/s" only works if encoder.getRate() really returns m/s. This is why Lesson 11's ticks→metres step has to be right first. Garbage units in, garbage feedforward out.
