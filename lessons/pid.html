The PID Controller

The single most-used control algorithm in the world. Three simple terms. Endless power.

If a machine corrects itself — a drone, a 3D printer, cruise control, a thermostat, a rocket — there's a very good chance a PID controller is doing it. It's the Swiss Army knife of control. And despite the intimidating name, it's just three reactions to the error added together:

🅿️

P — Proportional

"React to the error now." The bigger the gap, the harder I push. Simple and strong, but alone it tends to fall short or overshoot.

🅸

I — Integral

"React to the error's history." If a small gap won't go away, I keep adding up effort until it does. Kills stubborn, leftover error.

🅳

D — Derivative

"React to the error's trend." If I'm closing the gap fast, I ease off early so I don't overshoot. The brakes that smooth everything out.

The whole controller in one line

push = Kp·error + Ki·(sum of past errors) + Kd·(how fast error is changing)

The three gains Kp, Ki, Kd are the knobs you tune. Getting them right ("tuning") is the whole craft. Too little and it's sluggish; too much and it shakes itself apart. The fastest way to understand them is to break a drone, so let's do that.

Three experiments to really get it:
  1. Hit "P only". The drone hovers below the target forever — it needs constant thrust to fight gravity, which P can only make by keeping an error. This "droop" is called steady-state error.
  2. Now hit "PID (tuned)". The I term slowly builds up exactly enough thrust to cancel gravity, and the drone settles right on target. Watch the yellow I-meter climb.
  3. Hit "Too much Kp" and a wind gust. It overshoots and wobbles — too aggressive. The D term is what tames that.

Jittery, twitchyKd too high (amplifies noise)Lower Kd, filter the input
🔗
This is where filters meet controllers. The D term reacts to change, so it amplifies sensor noise badly. Real PID controllers run their measurement through a low-pass filter (Lesson 3!) first. Filters and controllers are teammates.
