Model Predictive Control

The controller that thinks before it acts — by simulating the future, over and over.

PID and bang-bang are reactive: they look at the error right now and respond. Model Predictive Control (MPC) is different — it's a planner. Before every move, it runs a little simulation of the future in its head, tries out different plans, picks the best one… then takes only the first step of that plan, and re-plans from scratch a moment later.

♟️ Chess analogy. A good chess player doesn't just react to the last move — they think "if I do this, then that, then…" several moves ahead, choose the best line, play one move, then re-think on the next turn as the board changes. MPC plays your system like a game of chess.

The three ingredients

🧠

A model

A formula that predicts "if I push this hard, where will I be next?" The "M" in MPC.

🔭

A horizon

How many steps into the future to plan. Longer = more foresight, but more computing.

⚖️

A cost

What "good" means: get close to the target, but don't waste effort or move violently.

Try this: set the horizon very short — the cart lunges and overshoots because it can't see far enough ahead to start braking in time. Now make the horizon long — it eases off the gas early and glides smoothly onto the target. Seeing further ahead = smoother control. That's the whole pitch of MPC.

Why use MPC instead of PID?

  • It can plan around limits. If a motor maxes out or a car can't exceed a speed, MPC bakes that into the plan instead of slamming into it.
  • It looks ahead. It can start braking before a turn, or anticipate a known future change — PID can only react once the error appears.
  • It handles many knobs at once. Steering + throttle + braking together, all balanced in one plan.
⚠️
The catch: MPC has to solve an optimization problem many times a second, so it needs real computing power and a good model. PID needs almost nothing. That's why your thermostat isn't MPC — but a self-driving car or a rocket landing very much is.
