Control & Feedback

The big idea behind every self-correcting machine — from a thermostat to a Mars lander.

Control theory is the science of making something behave the way you want. You have a goal (a room at 21°C, a drone hovering at 2 m, a car at 100 km/h) and you have a knob you can turn (a heater, a motor, the gas pedal). Control theory answers one question: how should I turn the knob, moment to moment, to hit my goal and stay there — even when the world pushes back?

🚿 Everyday analogy. Adjusting a shower is control theory. You feel the water (a measurement), compare it to "comfy" (your goal), and nudge the tap (your action). Too cold → turn toward hot. Too hot → back off. You repeat this loop a few times a second without thinking. That loop is the heart of everything here.

Open loop vs. closed loop

There are two ways to control something. The difference is whether the machine looks at the result and corrects itself.

✅
Open loop works only if nothing surprising happens (a microwave runs for 30 s no matter what). Closed loop (feedback) survives the real world — wind, friction, a heavier load — because it keeps watching and adjusting.
