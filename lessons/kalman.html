The Kalman Filter

The legendary filter that landed Apollo on the Moon — and lives in your phone's GPS.

The Kalman filter has a scary reputation, but the idea is beautifully simple. It's a filter that does two things over and over: it predicts where something should be, then corrects that guess with a fresh (noisy) measurement — blending the two in the smartest possible proportion. It even tracks how unsure it is and uses that to decide how much to trust the next reading.

🚗 Analogy. You're tracking a friend's car. You know its speed, so you can predict "it should be about here now." Then a blurry GPS ping arrives. You don't fully believe the ping (GPS is noisy) and you don't fully believe your guess (cars turn) — you split the difference, leaning toward whichever you trust more right now. That's a Kalman filter.

The two-step heartbeat

The secret sauce: the Kalman gain

The whole filter balances on one number, the Kalman gain (K) — basically "how much do I trust this new measurement?" It's computed automatically from the two uncertainties:

  • If the sensor is noisy → small gain → mostly trust the prediction (stay smooth).
  • If your prediction is shaky → big gain → jump toward the measurement.
  • It re-balances every single step. That self-tuning is what makes it special.
🔢
The update is just a smart version of the low-pass formula you already know:
estimate = prediction + K × (measurement − prediction)
The only new trick is that K is chosen optimally from the math, instead of you guessing an α by hand.

Look at the readouts: the Kalman error is usually a fraction of the raw blip error — it's genuinely extracting signal from noise. Push measurement noise way up and the blue line stays calm and sensible while the blips scatter wildly. That's the magic.
⚠️
Set Model trust very low and watch the estimate lag on sharp turns — it trusts its "moves predictably" assumption too much. Crank it high and the estimate gets jumpy, chasing every blip. Tuning that balance is the real job of a Kalman engineer.

One catch: the basic Kalman filter assumes everything moves in nice straight-line (linear) ways. The real world has curves, angles, and spin. For that we need a clever upgrade…
