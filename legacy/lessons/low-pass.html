The Low-Pass Filter

The simplest, most-used filter on Earth — one line of code that smooths almost anything.

A low-pass filter lets slow changes through and blocks fast ones. Since real signals usually wiggle slowly and noise wiggles fast, blocking the fast stuff throws away most of the noise. The name says it all: it lets low frequencies pass.

🪣 The leaky-bucket analogy. Picture a bucket with a small hole. You pour the noisy sensor readings in. The water level can't jump around instantly — it rises and falls gradually, ignoring quick splashes but following the overall trend. The water level is your smoothed output.

The one formula you need

Each new reading, you take a little step from your old estimate toward the new measurement. How big a step? A number α (alpha) between 0 and 1:

new estimate = old estimate + α × ( measurement − old estimate )

That "(measurement − old estimate)" part is just the error — how surprised you are by the new reading. You move a fraction α of the way toward the surprise.

α valueBehaviourFeels like
near 0Tiny steps → super smooth, but slow & laggyA heavy, calm flywheel
near 1Big steps → snappy, but barely filters anythingA twitchy, nervous needle
⚖️
The eternal trade-off: smoothness vs. lag. More smoothing always means more delay. There's no free lunch — you're choosing how much you trust the latest reading versus your accumulated history.

Where it's used

  • Smoothing a joystick or volume knob so it doesn't twitch.
  • Audio: removing hiss, taming treble (bass is "low-pass" by nature).
  • Cleaning up temperature, light, or battery readings on a microcontroller.
  • As a building block inside fancier filters (you'll meet it again in the complementary filter).
🧠
Bonus: swap the formula around and you get a high-pass filter — measurement − low-pass output — which keeps only the fast changes and throws away the slow ones. Useful for detecting sudden taps, beats, or edges.
