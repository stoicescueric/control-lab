Signals & Noise

Why every sensor lies a little — and why that's the whole reason filters exist.

A signal is just a value that changes over time: the temperature in a room, the height of a drone, the speed of a car. We can't see these true values directly — we read them with sensors. And here's the catch every engineer learns the hard way:

⚠️
Sensors never give you the truth. They give you the truth plus a little garbage — random wobble, spikes, and slow drift. That garbage is called noise.
measurement = true value + noise   ← what the sensor reports = what's real + random junk
🎧 Analogy. Imagine listening to a friend talk at a noisy party. Their words are the signal; the crowd is the noise. Your brain works hard to pull the words out of the racket. A filter does exactly that job for a sensor.

The three troublemakers

🌫️

Jitter (random noise)

Fast, small, random wobble around the true value. The most common kind.

⚡

Spikes (outliers)

Occasional crazy readings — a loose wire, a reflection, a glitch.

📉

Drift / bias

A slow, sneaky lean away from the truth that builds up over time.

Sampling: the world in tiny snapshots

Computers don't read sensors continuously — they take samples many times a second (the sample rate). Between samples, they're blind. Everything in this site works on these little snapshots, usually tens to hundreds per second. Each filter and controller just asks: given the snapshots so far, what's my best guess and my best move?
