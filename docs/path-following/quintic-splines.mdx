# Quintic & Bézier Splines

<Difficulty level="Hard" />

<Abstract>
There are two common ways to describe a smooth path: a Bézier curve, shaped by dragging control points
that pull the curve toward them, and a quintic Hermite spline, pinned to waypoints with specified
velocity and acceleration. Béziers are intuitive to author by feel; quintics pass exactly through your
points and reach continuous curvature by construction. This lesson builds both and shows when each wins.
</Abstract>

PID-to-point and predictive deceleration drive to *one* target. Real autonomous routines thread through
many waypoints, and the path *between* them has to be drivable — smooth not just in position but in how
sharply it bends. That bending is curvature, and curvature is where naive paths quietly fail.

<Problem title="The robot jerks at every waypoint">

Chain simple segments between waypoints and the path *looks* smooth: no kinks, continuous heading. But
run a follower along it and the robot snaps its steering at each join. The reason is invisible in the
drawing — **curvature** (how tightly the path turns, the second derivative) is *discontinuous* at the
waypoints. The follower must change steering angle instantly, which means infinite angular acceleration,
which the robot answers with a jerk and a tracking error exactly where the segments meet.

The cure is a curve description that controls curvature, not just position. Two families do it, from two
opposite philosophies.

</Problem>

<Theory title="Continuity: how smooth is smooth enough">

"Smoothness" is graded by how many derivatives match across a join:

| Class | Matches across the join | What stays continuous | The drivetrain feels |
|---|---|---|---|
| C⁰ | position | the path connects | a corner — a kink |
| C¹ | + first derivative | heading (tangent direction) | a sudden curvature change — a jerk |
| C² | + second derivative | curvature | smooth, followable steering |

C² is the target: continuous curvature means the follower never has to change steering instantly. Now the
two ways to get there.

### Bézier curves — shape by control points (approximation)

A cubic Bézier is built from four **control points** $P_0, P_1, P_2, P_3$ and a weighted blend called
the Bernstein basis:

$$
\mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)^3\,P_0 + 3(1-t)^2 t\,P_1 + 3(1-t)t^2\,P_2 + t^3\,P_3,
\qquad t \in [0,1]
$$

The weights always sum to $1$, so the curve stays inside the control polygon's hull. It passes through
**only the endpoints** $P_0$ and $P_3$; the interior points $P_1, P_2$ are *handles* that pull the curve
without touching it. They set the endpoint tangents directly:

$$
\mathbf{B}'(0) = 3(P_1 - P_0), \qquad \mathbf{B}'(1) = 3(P_3 - P_2)
$$

That is why Béziers feel good to author — drag a handle and the curve leans that way. The point on the
curve at any $t$ is found by **de Casteljau's algorithm**: repeatedly interpolate along the control
polygon. Scrub $t$ in the demo to watch it, and drag the handles to feel how $P_1, P_2$ steer the ends:

<BezierExplorer />

The catch shows up when you chain Béziers. Matching the tangent across a join (C¹) forces the next
segment's first handle to mirror the previous one, $Q_1 - Q_0 = P_3 - P_2$; demanding C² pins the second
handle too ($Q_2 = P_1 - 4P_2 + 4P_3$). So in a Bézier *chain*, continuity steals exactly the handles
that made Béziers convenient — you no longer get to place them freely.

### Quintic Hermite — pin waypoints and derivatives (interpolation)

The other philosophy: instead of points that pull the curve, specify the curve's own state at each
**waypoint** — position, velocity, and acceleration — and let one polynomial satisfy all of it. Because
you pin the values, the curve passes *through* every waypoint. This is the difference in one picture —
the same four points, used as Bézier control points versus Hermite waypoints:

<SplineCompare />

The green curve hits all four points; the blue one only the ends. A drivetrain routine usually wants the
green behavior: "go through these poses," not "go near them."

How many degrees of freedom does it take? Matching position, velocity, and acceleration at *both* ends of
a segment is six conditions, so you need a degree-5 — **quintic** — polynomial per coordinate:

$$
x(t) = c_5 t^5 + c_4 t^4 + c_3 t^3 + c_2 t^2 + c_1 t + c_0
$$

Evaluating it and its derivatives at $t = 0$ fixes the low coefficients straight from the start
conditions:

$$
c_0 = x(0), \qquad c_1 = x'(0), \qquad c_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}x''(0)
$$

and the endpoint-at-$t{=}1$ conditions give the top three:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} c_3 \\ c_4 \\ c_5 \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\phantom{-}10 & -4 & \phantom{-}\tfrac{1}{2} \\
-15 & \phantom{-}7 & -1 \\
\phantom{-}6 & -3 & \phantom{-}\tfrac{1}{2}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x(1) - c_0 - c_1 - c_2 \\
x'(1) - c_1 - 2c_2 \\
x''(1) - 2c_2
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Now the C² join is automatic: set each interior waypoint's ending acceleration equal to the next
segment's starting acceleration, $x''_\text{seg}|_{t=1} = x''_\text{next}|_{t=0}$, and the curvature is
continuous through the whole spline — no handles sacrificed.

### Natural cubic splines — interpolate every point, solve for the slopes

Quintic Hermite asks *you* for the velocity and acceleration at each waypoint. What if you only have the
points and want the smoothest curve through them? A **natural cubic spline** keeps one cubic per interval
but, instead of being handed the slopes, *solves for them* by demanding curvature match everywhere.

Lay a cubic $S_i(x)$ on each interval $[x_i, x_{i+1}]$ and impose:

- **C⁰** — each piece hits its endpoints, $S_i(x_i) = y_i$ and $S_i(x_{i+1}) = y_{i+1}$;
- **C¹ and C²** — neighbouring pieces share first *and* second derivative at every interior knot.

That is two conditions short. The **natural** boundary closes the gap by setting curvature to zero at the
two ends, $S''(x_0) = S''(x_n) = 0$ — the spline leaves the path straight, like a drafting spline
released at its tips.

Writing the unknowns as the second derivatives ("moments") $M_i = S''(x_i)$ collapses all of that into a
single **tridiagonal** system. With knot spacing $h_i = x_{i+1} - x_i$:

$$
h_{i-1} M_{i-1} + 2(h_{i-1} + h_i)\,M_i + h_i M_{i+1}
  = 6\!\left(\frac{y_{i+1} - y_i}{h_i} - \frac{y_i - y_{i-1}}{h_{i-1}}\right),
\qquad M_0 = M_n = 0
$$

Each row touches only three unknowns, so the **Thomas algorithm** solves the whole spline in $O(n)$ — no
general matrix inversion. With the moments in hand, the piece on $[x_i, x_{i+1}]$ is

$$
S_i(x) = \frac{M_i (x_{i+1}-x)^3 + M_{i+1}(x - x_i)^3}{6 h_i}
  + \left(\frac{y_i}{h_i} - \frac{M_i h_i}{6}\right)(x_{i+1}-x)
  + \left(\frac{y_{i+1}}{h_i} - \frac{M_{i+1} h_i}{6}\right)(x - x_i)
$$

The trade against quintic Hermite: a natural cubic is C² with the *fewest* degrees of freedom and needs
no slope input, but you don't get to set the endpoint curvature (it is pinned to zero), and the curvature
— though continuous — has a corner in its *own* derivative. Quintic buys that extra smoothness with two
more coefficients per segment.

Drag the waypoints below: the spline always threads through every point, the curvature comb stays smooth
across each knot (that is the C²), and it flattens to nothing at the two ends — the natural boundary
$M_0 = M_n = 0$ in action.

<NaturalCubicSpline />

<Callout type="warning" title="A path is not a function">
$S(x)$ needs $x$ to increase monotonically — a robot path that doubles back has two $y$'s for one $x$.
Fit **two** natural cubic splines instead, $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ against the point index (or arc length) $t$,
and evaluate them together. The same fix applies to any interpolating spline used as a 2-D trajectory.
</Callout>

<SplineContinuityIllustration />

Whichever family you use, the quantity a [pure-pursuit](./pure-pursuit.mdx) or feedforward follower
actually consumes is the **curvature** at each sample:

$$
\kappa(t) = \frac{x'y'' - y'x''}{\left(x'^2 + y'^2\right)^{3/2}}
$$

<Callout type="idea" title="Which one should you use?">
**Bézier** when a human authors the path by eye — the handles are a great editor, and the Road Runner /
Pedro Pathing-style tools expose them. **Quintic Hermite** when you have exact poses to hit (Road
Runner's `splineTo` is quintic under the hood) and want C² for free. **Natural cubic** when you have only
points (no slopes) and want the cheapest C² interpolation — one tridiagonal solve, at the cost of pinned
(zero) end curvature. Bézier and Hermite are convertible: a cubic Bézier *is* a cubic Hermite with
$m_0 = 3(P_1-P_0)$, $m_1 = 3(P_3-P_2)$ — so editors can show handles while the math runs on Hermite
coefficients.
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Evaluate either curve on the Control Hub">

Both reduce to evaluating a polynomial (and its derivatives) per coordinate each loop.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Cubic Bézier and quintic Hermite segments">{`/** Cubic Bézier in one coordinate: 4 control points, t in [0,1]. */
public static double bezier(double p0, double p1, double p2, double p3, double t) {
    double u = 1 - t;
    return u*u*u*p0 + 3*u*u*t*p1 + 3*u*t*t*p2 + t*t*t*p3;
}

/** Quintic Hermite segment: pinned position/velocity/accel at both ends. */
public final class Quintic {
    private final double c0, c1, c2, c3, c4, c5;

    public Quintic(double p0, double v0, double a0, double p1, double v1, double a1) {
        c0 = p0;
        c1 = v0;
        c2 = 0.5 * a0;
        double d0 = p1 - c0 - c1 - c2;   // residuals the top coeffs must absorb
        double d1 = v1 - c1 - 2 * c2;
        double d2 = a1 - 2 * c2;
        c3 =  10 * d0 - 4 * d1 + 0.5 * d2;
        c4 = -15 * d0 + 7 * d1 - 1.0 * d2;
        c5 =   6 * d0 - 3 * d1 + 0.5 * d2;
    }

    public double pos(double t) { return ((((c5*t + c4)*t + c3)*t + c2)*t + c1)*t + c0; }
    public double vel(double t) { return (((5*c5*t + 4*c4)*t + 3*c3)*t + 2*c2)*t + c1; }
    public double acc(double t) { return ((20*c5*t + 12*c4)*t + 6*c3)*t + 2*c2; }
}`}</JavaCode>

Build one curve for $x$ and one for $y$ per segment. For a quintic chain, sharing endpoint velocity and
acceleration between adjacent segments is what makes the whole path C².

For a **natural cubic** you don't build the slopes — you solve for the moments with one tridiagonal
sweep, then read off each piece:

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Natural cubic spline: solve the tridiagonal moment system">{`/** Second derivatives (moments) for a natural cubic spline through (x[i], y[i]). */
static double[] naturalCubicMoments(double[] x, double[] y) {
    int n = x.length;
    double[] h = new double[n - 1];
    for (int i = 0; i < n - 1; i++) h[i] = x[i + 1] - x[i];

    // tridiagonal rows: sub[i]*M[i-1] + diag[i]*M[i] + sup[i]*M[i+1] = rhs[i]
    double[] diag = new double[n], sup = new double[n], sub = new double[n], rhs = new double[n];
    diag[0] = diag[n - 1] = 1;                       // natural boundary: M[0] = M[n-1] = 0
    for (int i = 1; i < n - 1; i++) {
        sub[i]  = h[i - 1];
        diag[i] = 2 * (h[i - 1] + h[i]);
        sup[i]  = h[i];
        rhs[i]  = 6 * ((y[i + 1] - y[i]) / h[i] - (y[i] - y[i - 1]) / h[i - 1]);
    }

    // Thomas algorithm: forward sweep, then back substitution -- O(n)
    for (int i = 1; i < n; i++) {
        double m = sub[i] / diag[i - 1];
        diag[i] -= m * sup[i - 1];
        rhs[i]  -= m * rhs[i - 1];
    }
    double[] M = new double[n];
    M[n - 1] = rhs[n - 1] / diag[n - 1];
    for (int i = n - 2; i >= 0; i--) M[i] = (rhs[i] - sup[i] * M[i + 1]) / diag[i];
    return M;   // feed M[i], M[i+1] into the S_i(x) formula above to evaluate
}`}</JavaCode>

For a 2-D path, run this twice — once for $x(t)$ and once for $y(t)$ against the point index $t$.

### Following the spline

You don't follow a spline as a continuous function — you **resample it into a list of points** (the
arc-length step the warning below insists on), tag each point with its curvature radius $R = 1/\kappa$,
then hand that list to PID-to-point's [point-advance follower](./pid-to-point.mdx). Two spline-specific
refinements turn that into a fast, accurate follow:

**Speed from curvature.** The robot can corner only as hard as traction allows. The lateral acceleration
on a curve is $a_\text{lat} = v^2/R$, so capping it at the slip limit gives a per-point speed:

$$
v \le \sqrt{a_\text{lat}^\text{max}\, R}
$$

Big $R$ (gentle) ⇒ full speed; small $R$ (tight) ⇒ slow down. (FTCclueless uses a cheaper ramp to the
same effect, `speed = 0.25 + 0.75·min(|R|, 200)/200`.)

**Centripetal feedforward.** To *stay* on a curve of radius $R$ at speed $v$, the robot needs a sideways
(centripetal) acceleration toward the center; without it, feedback alone always lags and the robot drifts
to the outside of every turn. Add it as a strafe feedforward:

$$
u_\text{strafe} \mathrel{+}= k_c\,\frac{v^2}{R}
$$

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Spline follower: resample, then curvature speed + centripetal">{`/** A sampled point along the spline. */
record SplinePoint(double x, double y, double heading, double radius) {}

/** Resample the spline by arc length; tag each point with its curvature radius. */
List<SplinePoint> resample(Spline s, double stepInches) {
    List<SplinePoint> pts = new ArrayList<>();
    for (double d = 0; d <= s.length(); d += stepInches) {
        double t = s.tAtDistance(d);                 // arc-length -> parameter
        double dx = s.dx(t),  dy = s.dy(t);          // first derivatives
        double ddx = s.ddx(t), ddy = s.ddy(t);       // second derivatives
        double kappa = (dx*ddy - dy*ddx) / Math.pow(dx*dx + dy*dy, 1.5);
        double radius = Math.abs(kappa) < 1e-6 ? 1e6 : 1.0 / kappa;
        pts.add(new SplinePoint(s.x(t), s.y(t), Math.atan2(dy, dx), radius));
    }
    return pts;
}

/** One loop: point-advance to the target, scale speed by curvature, add centripetal FF. */
public void followSpline(List<SplinePoint> path) {
    Pose2d robot = localizer.getPose();
    while (index < path.size() - 1 &&
           Math.hypot(path.get(index).x() - robot.x, path.get(index).y() - robot.y) < PATH_RADIUS) {
        index++;
    }
    SplinePoint p = path.get(index);

    double speed = Math.min(MAX_SPEED, Math.sqrt(LAT_ACCEL * Math.abs(p.radius())));

    // robot-frame direction to the point -> forward / strafe at the curvature-limited speed
    double ex = p.x() - robot.x, ey = p.y() - robot.y;
    double c = Math.cos(robot.heading), s = Math.sin(robot.heading);
    double dir = Math.atan2(ey * c - ex * s, ex * c + ey * s);
    double vx = speed * Math.cos(dir);
    double vy = speed * Math.sin(dir);

    vy += K_CENTRIPETAL * speed * speed / p.radius();   // hold the curve, don't get flung out

    drive(vx, vy, headingController(p.heading()));        // mecanum mixer desaturates
}`}</JavaCode>

This is FTCclueless's spline follower in miniature: resample with a per-point radius, advance through the
points, slow for tight curvature, and feed forward the centripetal term so the follower tracks the curve
instead of cutting it.

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: t is not distance">

For both curves, the parameter $t$ is not arc length — equal steps in $t$ are unequal steps in metres,
fast on straights and bunched in tight turns. Before a follower uses the path, **reparameterize by arc
length** (integrate $\sqrt{x'^2 + y'^2}$ and resample) or your speed control will surge and stall around
curves.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="Why C² matters for the speed plan">

The curvature speed cap above is only as smooth as $\kappa$ itself. With a C¹ cubic path, $\kappa$ jumps
at every waypoint, so the *speed limit* jumps too and the robot lurches. C² continuity from a quintic
keeps the curvature — and therefore the whole speed profile — continuous.

</Admonition>
