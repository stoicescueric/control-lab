# Mecanum Kinematics

<Abstract>
A mecanum drivetrain converts one desired chassis motion — forward, strafe, and turn — into four
independent wheel powers through a fixed linear map. This lesson builds that map from the rollers up,
adds the field-centric rotation and joystick shaping drivers actually want, and shows why the
desaturation step is what keeps a "full speed" command driving in a straight line.
</Abstract>

Mecanum wheels are the reason an FTC robot can slide sideways without turning. Each wheel has rollers
canted at 45°, so the wheel's grip points diagonally. Drive the four wheels in the right *combination*
and those diagonal forces add up to pure forward, pure sideways, pure rotation, or anything between. The
whole skill is choosing that combination — and it is exactly one matrix multiply.

<Problem title="One driver intent, four motors, every loop">

The driver gives you three numbers from two sticks: how much to go **forward**, how much to **strafe**,
and how much to **turn**. The robot has four motors. Somewhere in your `OpMode` you must turn three
numbers into four, sixty times a second, and get the signs right — or the robot strafes when it should
spin.

Worse, the obvious "just add the joystick axes" code has two silent bugs. It drives relative to the
*robot*, so a disoriented driver fights the controls the moment the robot turns; and when you push two
sticks at once it asks a wheel for more than 100% power, which the hardware quietly clips — turning your
straight-line command into a curve.

</Problem>

<Theory title="From rollers to a wheel-mixing matrix">

Work in the robot's own frame first. Call the chassis command

$$
\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

where $v_x$ is forward speed, $v_y$ is strafe speed (positive to the **left**), and $\omega$ is turn
rate (positive **counter-clockwise**). Because of the 45° rollers, each wheel responds to forward and
strafe with opposite signs on the diagonal, and to rotation by how far it sits from the center. Writing
that out for the four wheels — front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right — gives the **mecanum
inverse-kinematics matrix**:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} w_{FL} \\ w_{FR} \\ w_{BL} \\ w_{BR} \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 & -(L+W) \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & \phantom{-}(L+W) \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & -(L+W) \\
1 & -1 & \phantom{-}(L+W)
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

Here $L$ and $W$ are the distances from the robot center to the wheel axles (front-to-back and
side-to-side); the term $L+W$ sets how strongly turning loads each wheel. In normalized teleop you can
fold those constants into $\omega$ and just read off the pattern:

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_{FL} &= v_x - v_y - \omega &
w_{FR} &= v_x + v_y + \omega \\
w_{BL} &= v_x + v_y - \omega &
w_{BR} &= v_x - v_y + \omega
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the same idea as the [linear-algebra preface](../preface/why-math-matters/linear-algebra.mdx):
a single matrix is the clean way to combine forward, strafe, and turn instead of four hand-tuned
formulas.

### Field-centric: rotate the command first

Drivers think in *field* directions — "go to the scoring side" — not robot directions. So before the
matrix, rotate the driver's field command into the robot frame by the robot's heading $\theta$ (read
from the IMU). This is the rotation from the linear-algebra lesson, by $-\theta$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}_\text{robot}
=
\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}_\text{field}
$$

Now "push the stick away from you" means the same field direction no matter which way the robot faces.

### Joystick response curves

A raw stick is linear, but fine alignment near the goal needs gentle low-stick response while still
reaching full speed at the top. Shape the **magnitude** (so you don't distort the direction) by raising
it to a power $k \ge 1$:

$$
m_\text{out} = m_\text{in}^{\,k}, \qquad m_\text{in} = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}
$$

$k = 1$ is linear; $k = 2$ (squared) is the classic FTC feel — half stick gives a quarter speed, so
slow moves are precise and the top end is untouched.

### Desaturation: the step everyone forgets

The matrix can hand back a wheel power above $1.0$. If you just clip it, the *other* wheels keep their
values and the ratio between wheels changes — so the robot curves away from the commanded direction. The
fix is to scale **all four together** by the largest magnitude, but only if it exceeds one:

$$
\text{scale} = \max\big(1,\ |w_{FL}|, |w_{FR}|, |w_{BL}|, |w_{BR}|\big),
\qquad
w_i \leftarrow \frac{w_i}{\text{scale}}
$$

Small commands pass through untouched; oversized ones shrink while **keeping their ratios**, so the
direction of travel is preserved. Drive the demo's forward and turn sticks both to the top and watch the
"desaturate ÷" readout climb above 1 — that divisor is the only thing keeping the robot honest.

<Deploy title="A clean field-centric teleop drive">

The whole pipeline is short when you respect the order: **shape → rotate → mix → desaturate.**

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Field-centric mecanum with response curve + desaturation">{`/** Drives a mecanum chassis from field-relative stick input.
 *  @param fwd   forward command, +1 away from the driver   [-1, 1]
 *  @param strafe strafe command, +1 to the left             [-1, 1]
 *  @param turn  turn command, +1 counter-clockwise          [-1, 1]
 *  @param heading robot heading from the IMU, radians (field frame)
 */
public void driveFieldCentric(double fwd, double strafe, double turn, double heading) {
    // 1) response curve on the translation magnitude (direction preserved)
    double mag = Math.min(1.0, Math.hypot(fwd, strafe));
    double dir = Math.atan2(strafe, fwd);
    double curved = Math.pow(mag, DRIVE_CURVE);          // DRIVE_CURVE = 2.0 feels good
    double fwdC = curved * Math.cos(dir);
    double strC = curved * Math.sin(dir);

    // 2) rotate the field command into the robot frame (rotate by -heading)
    double cos = Math.cos(heading), sin = Math.sin(heading);
    double vx =  fwdC * cos + strC * sin;
    double vy = -fwdC * sin + strC * cos;
    double w  = turn;

    // 3) wheel-mixing matrix
    double fl = vx - vy - w;
    double fr = vx + vy + w;
    double bl = vx + vy - w;
    double br = vx - vy + w;

    // 4) desaturate: scale all four down together if any exceeds 1.0
    double scale = Math.max(1.0,
        Math.max(Math.abs(fl), Math.max(Math.abs(fr),
        Math.max(Math.abs(bl), Math.abs(br)))));
    frontLeft.setPower(fl / scale);
    frontRight.setPower(fr / scale);
    backLeft.setPower(bl / scale);
    backRight.setPower(br / scale);
}`}</JavaCode>

With teleop solid, the same chassis command becomes the *output* of an autonomous controller. The
simplest one drives straight at a target pose — that is [**PID-to-point**](./pid-to-point.mdx), the next
lesson.

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: mecanum is not a precise odometry source">

Those canted rollers slip sideways by design, so wheel encoders on a mecanum drive over-report strafe
distance and drift fast. Use the drive for *motion*, but localize from dedicated dead-wheel pods or
fused vision — see [Odometry & Pose](../localization-odometry/odometry.mdx).

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="Sign conventions are the #1 bug">

If a wheel fights the others, flip its motor direction, not the math. Pick one convention (+x forward,
+y left, +ω CCW), write it in a comment, and make every wheel obey it. Half of all "mecanum is broken"
posts are one inverted motor.

</Admonition>
