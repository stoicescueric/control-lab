# Mecanum Kinematics

<Difficulty level="Easy" />

<Abstract>
A mecanum drive can slide in any direction without turning. This lesson shows the one idea behind it —
each wheel can only push along a 45° diagonal, so the right *combination* of four wheels makes any motion
— then turns that into the handful of lines of code you actually write: mix the command into four wheel
powers, rotate it for field-centric driving, scale the joystick for fine control, and add a dead zone so
a twitchy stick doesn't creep the robot.
</Abstract>

Good news: this is one of the easier topics in the whole curriculum. There is no calculus and no big
matrix to invert — just some adding and subtracting, done sixty times a second. By the end you will be
able to write a clean teleop drive from memory.

<Problem title="Three numbers in, four motors out">

The driver gives you three numbers from two thumbsticks: how much to go **forward**, how much to
**strafe** (slide sideways), and how much to **turn**. Your robot has four motors. Every loop you have to
turn those three numbers into four wheel powers — and get the signs right, or the robot strafes when you
meant to spin.

There are also two traps the naïve "just add the sticks together" code falls into: it drives relative to
the *robot*, so a turned-around driver fights the controls; and when you push two sticks at once it asks a
wheel for more than 100% power, which the motor silently clips — bending your straight line into a curve.
We'll fix both.

</Problem>

<Theory title="One idea: each wheel pushes along a diagonal">

A mecanum wheel has little rollers wrapped around it at **45°**. Because of that angle, when the wheel
spins it doesn't grip straight forward like a normal wheel — it grips along a **diagonal**. The four
wheels are mirrored so their diagonals form an **X** when you look down at the robot.

That one fact is the whole lesson. A single wheel can only push along its diagonal, but *add up* the four
diagonal pushes and the sideways parts can cancel (giving pure forward), or the forward parts can cancel
(giving pure strafe), or they can reinforce into a spin. Choosing the four wheel speeds to get the motion
you want is the only skill here — and it's just addition.

### Mixing: add the three things you want

Work in the robot's own frame first. Call the command

$$
\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

where $v_x$ is forward speed, $v_y$ is strafe speed (positive to the **left**), and $\omega$ is turn rate
(positive **counter-clockwise**). Each wheel gets a share of all three. Writing out the four wheels —
front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right:

$$
\begin{aligned}
w_{FL} &= v_x - v_y - \omega &
w_{FR} &= v_x + v_y + \omega \\
w_{BL} &= v_x + v_y - \omega &
w_{BR} &= v_x - v_y + \omega
\end{aligned}
$$

Read it one column at a time and it's obvious:

- **Forward** ($v_x$) adds to **all four** equally → the robot drives straight.
- **Strafe** ($v_y$) adds to one diagonal (FR, BL) and subtracts from the other (FL, BR) → the X-pattern
  pushes the robot sideways.
- **Turn** ($\omega$) adds to the **right** wheels and subtracts from the **left** → the robot spins in
  place.

That's the same thing as one matrix multiply, which is the compact way to write it (and how the ECAM and
gm0 references at the end of this lesson present it):

$$
\begin{bmatrix} w_{FL} \\ w_{FR} \\ w_{BL} \\ w_{BR} \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 & -1 \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & \phantom{-}1 \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & -1 \\
1 & -1 & \phantom{-}1
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

distances from the robot's center to the wheels — it just says wheels farther out get loaded more when
turning. For normalized teleop you can fold those constants into $\omega$ and use the clean $\pm 1$
pattern above. Push the sticks in the demo and watch each wheel's number come straight from that sum:

### Field-centric: rotate the command first

Drivers think in **field** directions — "go toward the goal" — not robot directions. So *before* the
mixing step, rotate the driver's field command into the robot's frame by the robot's heading $\theta$
(read from the IMU). It's a 2-D rotation by $-\theta$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}_\text{robot}
=
\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}_\text{field}
$$

### Desaturation: keep the robot driving straight

The mixing sum can hand back a wheel power bigger than $1.0$ — for example, full forward *and* full turn
asks the front-right wheel for $v_x + v_y + \omega = $ way over 100%. If you just clip that one wheel to
$1.0$, the *other* wheels keep their values, the ratios between wheels change, and the robot curves away
from where you aimed.

The fix is to scale **all four together** by the largest magnitude, but only when it exceeds one:

$$
\text{scale} = \max\big(1,\ |w_{FL}|, |w_{FR}|, |w_{BL}|, |w_{BR}|\big),
\qquad w_i \leftarrow \frac{w_i}{\text{scale}}
$$

Small commands pass through untouched; oversized ones shrink while **keeping their ratios**, so the
direction of travel is preserved. (gm0's tutorial does the same job with a single divisor
$\,\max(|v_x| + |v_y| + |\omega|,\ 1)\,$ — a slightly more conservative version of the same idea.) In the
demo, push forward and turn to the top and watch the "desaturate ÷" readout climb above 1 — that divisor
is the only thing keeping the robot honest.

### Scaling the joystick for fine control

A raw stick is **linear**: half stick gives half speed. But the moments you need precision — nudging up to
score — are exactly the small-stick moments, and linear response makes them twitchy. The fix is to bend
the response so small pushes give *very* small speeds while full stick still reaches full speed. Raise the
**magnitude** to a power $k \ge 1$ (shaping the magnitude, not each axis, so you don't distort the
direction):

$$
m_\text{out} = m_\text{in}^{\,k}, \qquad m_\text{in} = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}
$$

$k = 1$ is linear; $k = 2$ (squared) is the classic FTC feel — half stick now gives a quarter speed, so
slow moves are gentle and the top end is untouched. Some drivers prefer cubic ($k = 3$).

### Dead zone: ignore a stick that won't sit still

Thumbsticks don't return to exactly zero. Let go and the stick reports something like $0.03$ forever —
so the robot **creeps** on its own. A **dead zone** fixes it: any stick value smaller than a threshold
$d$ counts as zero.

But a plain dead zone has a side effect — the instant you cross $d$, the output jumps from $0$ to $d$,
giving a little lurch. The clean version **re-expands** the leftover range so the output rises smoothly
from $0$ at the edge of the dead zone up to $1$ at full stick. Combine it with the response curve and you
get one tidy shaping function:

$$
\text{shape}(s) =
\begin{cases}
0 & |s| \le d \\[4pt]
\operatorname{sign}(s)\,\left(\dfrac{|s| - d}{1 - d}\right)^{\!k} & |s| > d
\end{cases}
$$

Drag the input below: the flat red band is the dead zone (output stays $0$), and past it the blue curve
is your scaled response climbing to full. A dead zone of $0.05$–$0.10$ kills drift without you feeling it.

<Deploy title="A clean field-centric teleop drive">

The whole pipeline is short when you respect the order: **shape → rotate → mix → desaturate.**

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Field-centric mecanum with dead zone, response curve, and desaturation">{`/** Dead zone + response curve in one step: 0 inside the dead zone, then a
 *  smoothly re-expanded, curved response out to full stick. */
private double shape(double s) {
    double a = Math.abs(s);
    if (a < DEADZONE) return 0;                          // ignore stick drift
    double scaled = (a - DEADZONE) / (1 - DEADZONE);     // re-expand: 0 at the edge, 1 at full
    return Math.signum(s) * Math.pow(scaled, DRIVE_CURVE);  // DEADZONE~0.06, DRIVE_CURVE~2.0
}

/** Drives a mecanum chassis from field-relative stick input.
 *  @param fwd     forward command, +1 away from the driver  [-1, 1]
 *  @param strafe  strafe command,  +1 to the left           [-1, 1]
 *  @param turn    turn command,    +1 counter-clockwise      [-1, 1]
 *  @param heading robot heading from the IMU, radians (field frame)
 */
public void driveFieldCentric(double fwd, double strafe, double turn, double heading) {
    // 1) dead zone + response curve on the translation MAGNITUDE (direction preserved)
    double mag = Math.min(1.0, Math.hypot(fwd, strafe));
    double dir = Math.atan2(strafe, fwd);
    double m   = shape(mag);
    double fwdC = m * Math.cos(dir);
    double strC = m * Math.sin(dir);
    double w    = shape(turn);                 // same shaping on the turn stick

    // 2) rotate the field command into the robot frame (rotate by -heading)
    double cos = Math.cos(heading), sin = Math.sin(heading);
    double vx =  fwdC * cos + strC * sin;
    double vy = -fwdC * sin + strC * cos;

    // 3) wheel-mixing: add the three things you want
    double fl = vx - vy - w;
    double fr = vx + vy + w;
    double bl = vx + vy - w;
    double br = vx - vy + w;

    // 4) desaturate: scale all four down together if any exceeds 1.0
    double scale = Math.max(1.0,
        Math.max(Math.abs(fl), Math.max(Math.abs(fr),
        Math.max(Math.abs(bl), Math.abs(br)))));
    frontLeft.setPower(fl / scale);
    frontRight.setPower(fr / scale);
    backLeft.setPower(bl / scale);
    backRight.setPower(br / scale);
}`}</JavaCode>

In an FTC `OpMode` the sticks map in like this (the Y axis is inverted on the gamepad, so negate it):

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Reading the gamepad">{`double fwd    = -gamepad1.left_stick_y;   // up on the stick = forward
double strafe = -gamepad1.left_stick_x;   // left on the stick = +left (our convention)
double turn   = -gamepad1.right_stick_x;  // left = counter-clockwise
driveFieldCentric(fwd, strafe, turn, imu.getHeading());`}</JavaCode>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: strafing is weaker than driving">

Those canted rollers lose a little grip sideways, so a mecanum robot strafes *slower* than it drives
forward at the same power. The common fix (straight from gm0) is to multiply the strafe command by about
**1.1** before mixing, so sideways and forward feel matched. Tune the exact number on your robot.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: don't navigate from mecanum encoders">

The same sideways slip means wheel encoders on a mecanum drive over-report strafe distance and drift
fast. Use the drive for *motion*, but localize from dedicated dead-wheel pods or fused vision — see
[Odometry & Pose](../localization-odometry/odometry.mdx).

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="Sign conventions are the #1 bug">

If a wheel fights the others, flip its motor direction, not the math. Pick one convention (+x forward,
+y left, +ω CCW), write it in a comment, and make every wheel obey it. Half of all "mecanum is broken"
posts are one inverted motor.

</Admonition>
