# Inverse Kinematics

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Inverse kinematics answers the practical robotics question: "I know where I want the mechanism to go;
what motor positions or wheel speeds make that happen?" In FTC, that shows up in obvious places like
arms and turrets, but also in less obvious places like mecanum wheel mixing, where a desired chassis
motion becomes four wheel powers.
</Abstract>

A robot is built from actuators, gears, links, wheels, and sensors. A driver or autonomous routine does
not naturally think in those actuator coordinates. It thinks in goals: put the claw here, point the wrist
there, drive the robot sideways, rotate toward the backdrop, place the pixel at this height.

That mismatch is the reason inverse kinematics matters. It is the translation layer between **task
space** and **actuator space**:

$$
\text{desired motion in the world}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{motor commands that create it}
$$

For a drivetrain, task space might be a chassis velocity:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

where $v_x$ is forward speed, $v_y$ is sideways speed, and $\omega$ is angular velocity. For an arm, task
space might be a claw position:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}
$$

where $x$ is horizontal position and $y$ is vertical position measured from the shoulder pivot. In both
cases, the robot needs the same kind of answer: what should the motors do?

## First ask: how many freedoms does the mechanism have?

The Quality Control workshop makes a useful point before any equations appear: inverse kinematics starts
with **degree-of-freedom analysis**. A degree of freedom is one independent way something can move. A slide
has one translational degree of freedom. A simple arm joint has one rotational degree of freedom. A
turreted arm has at least two: turret angle plus arm angle. A claw that must stay level adds another
constraint.

Compare the degrees of freedom in the **target** with the degrees of freedom in the **mechanism**:

| Case | What it means on a robot | What the solver must do |
|---|---|---|
| Too few mechanism freedoms | A one-joint arm cannot place a claw at every $(x, y)$ point. | Reject unreachable targets or redesign the mechanism. |
| Same number of freedoms | A two-link arm can often solve $(x, y)$ with geometry. | Use a closed-form trig/vector solution when possible. |
| Extra mechanism freedoms | A turret + arm + slide may have many ways to reach the same point. | Choose a configuration using constraints, preferences, or numerical IK. |

This is why "the IK answer" is not always a single answer. Sometimes there are two valid answers
(elbow-up and elbow-down). Sometimes there are infinitely many, and the software must pick the one that
avoids collisions, respects joint limits, keeps the claw level, and does not jerk the mechanism between
two distant configurations.

<Theory title="Forward kinematics: angles to position">

It is easier to understand inverse kinematics if we first write the opposite direction. **Forward
kinematics** means: if the joint angles are known, where is the claw?

For a two-link arm:

- $L_1$ is the length of the first link, from the shoulder pivot to the elbow pivot.
- $L_2$ is the length of the second link, from the elbow pivot to the claw or wrist.
- $\theta_1$ is the shoulder angle, measured from the positive $x$ axis.
- $\theta_2$ is the elbow angle, measured relative to the first link.
- $x$ is the claw's horizontal position relative to the shoulder pivot.
- $y$ is the claw's vertical position relative to the shoulder pivot.

The first link contributes:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1 &= L_1\cos(\theta_1) \\
y_1 &= L_1\sin(\theta_1)
\end{aligned}
$$

The second link points at angle $\theta_1 + \theta_2$, because it is mounted on the end of the first link:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_2 &= L_2\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) \\
y_2 &= L_2\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)
\end{aligned}
$$

Add the two link vectors and you get the claw position:

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
x &= L_1\cos(\theta_1) + L_2\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) \\
y &= L_1\sin(\theta_1) + L_2\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)
\end{aligned}
}
$$

That is a simulation equation. Give it angles, and it tells you where the mechanism is.

### Inverse kinematics: position to angles

FTC code usually needs the reverse. The autonomous routine says "place the claw at $(x, y)$." The motors
need angles.

Draw a triangle from the shoulder to the elbow to the claw. The distance from the shoulder to the target
is:

$$
r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
$$

where $r$ is the straight-line distance from the shoulder pivot to the desired claw position. If $r$ is
greater than $L_1 + L_2$, the point is too far away. If $r$ is less than $|L_1 - L_2|$, the point is too
close to reach because one link cannot fold through the other.

The elbow angle comes from the law of cosines:

$$
\boxed{
\cos(\theta_2) =
\frac{r^2 - L_1^2 - L_2^2}{2L_1L_2}
}
$$

Then:

$$
\theta_2 = \pm \cos^{-1}\!\left(
\frac{r^2 - L_1^2 - L_2^2}{2L_1L_2}
\right)
$$

The $\pm$ is not decoration. It is the difference between the elbow-up and elbow-down solutions. Both may
put the claw at the same point, but one might collide with the robot frame while the other clears it.

To find the shoulder angle, point from the shoulder to the target, then subtract the angle inside the
link triangle:

$$
\boxed{
\theta_1 =
\operatorname{atan2}(y, x)
-
\operatorname{atan2}\!\left(
L_2\sin(\theta_2),
L_1 + L_2\cos(\theta_2)
\right)
}
$$

Here:

- $\operatorname{atan2}(y, x)$ is the field angle from the shoulder to the target.
- $L_2\sin(\theta_2)$ is the vertical component of the second link relative to the first.
- $L_1 + L_2\cos(\theta_2)$ is the horizontal reach of the two-link triangle along the first link.
- The second `atan2` subtracts the triangle's internal offset so the shoulder points to the correct
  elbow position, not directly at the claw.

### The drivetrain version

The same idea appears in mecanum. In [Mecanum Kinematics](./mecanum-kinematics.mdx), the desired chassis
motion is:

$$
\mathbf{u} =
\begin{bmatrix}
v_x \\
v_y \\
\omega
\end{bmatrix}
$$

where $v_x$ is forward velocity, $v_y$ is sideways velocity, and $\omega$ is turn rate. The wheel powers
are:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} w_{FL} \\ w_{FR} \\ w_{BL} \\ w_{BR} \end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 & -1 \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & \phantom{-}1 \\
1 & \phantom{-}1 & -1 \\
1 & -1 & \phantom{-}1
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \\ \omega \end{bmatrix}
$$

That is also inverse kinematics: a desired robot motion becomes actuator commands. The only difference is
that the actuator commands are wheel speeds instead of joint angles.

</Theory>

<Deploy title="A clean FTC implementation">

Keep inverse kinematics out of your OpMode. Your OpMode should ask for a target; a kinematics class
should return the joint angles. That separation makes the math testable and keeps hardware code from
turning into a geometry notebook.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Two-link arm inverse kinematics">{`/**
 * Inverse kinematics for a planar two-link arm.
 *
 * <p>Units are arbitrary but must be consistent: if link lengths are inches,
 * x and y targets must also be inches. Angles are returned in radians so they
 * can be passed directly into Math.sin/cos or converted to encoder targets.</p>
 */
public final class TwoLinkArmKinematics {
    private final double link1;
    private final double link2;

    public TwoLinkArmKinematics(double link1, double link2) {
        if (link1 <= 0 || link2 <= 0) {
            throw new IllegalArgumentException("Link lengths must be positive.");
        }
        this.link1 = link1;
        this.link2 = link2;
    }

    /**
     * Solves for the shoulder and elbow angles that place the wrist at (x, y).
     *
     * @param x horizontal target position from the shoulder pivot
     * @param y vertical target position from the shoulder pivot
     * @param elbowUp true for the elbow-up solution, false for elbow-down
     * @return an immutable result containing angles and reachability
     */
    public ArmAngles solve(double x, double y, boolean elbowUp) {
        double r = Math.hypot(x, y);
        double minReach = Math.abs(link1 - link2);
        double maxReach = link1 + link2;
        boolean reachable = r >= minReach && r <= maxReach;

        double cosElbow = (r * r - link1 * link1 - link2 * link2) / (2.0 * link1 * link2);
        cosElbow = clamp(cosElbow, -1.0, 1.0);

        double sinElbowMagnitude = Math.sqrt(Math.max(0.0, 1.0 - cosElbow * cosElbow));
        double sinElbow = elbowUp ? sinElbowMagnitude : -sinElbowMagnitude;

        double elbowRad = Math.atan2(sinElbow, cosElbow);
        double shoulderRad = Math.atan2(y, x)
            - Math.atan2(link2 * sinElbow, link1 + link2 * cosElbow);

        return new ArmAngles(shoulderRad, elbowRad, reachable);
    }

    private static double clamp(double value, double min, double max) {
        return Math.max(min, Math.min(max, value));
    }

    /** Immutable solution returned by the inverse-kinematics solver. */
    public static final class ArmAngles {
        public final double shoulderRad;
        public final double elbowRad;
        public final boolean reachable;

        public ArmAngles(double shoulderRad, double elbowRad, boolean reachable) {
            this.shoulderRad = shoulderRad;
            this.elbowRad = elbowRad;
            this.reachable = reachable;
        }
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

Then convert angles into your actual mechanism commands. That layer is robot-specific: encoder offsets,
gear ratios, feedforward, joint limits, and homing sensors belong there, not inside the geometry solver.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Using the solver inside a subsystem">{`public void moveWristTo(double xInches, double yInches) {
    TwoLinkArmKinematics.ArmAngles target = kinematics.solve(xInches, yInches, true);

    if (!target.reachable) {
        telemetry.addLine("Arm target outside reachable workspace.");
        return;
    }

    shoulderController.setTargetPosition(toShoulderTicks(target.shoulderRad));
    elbowController.setTargetPosition(toElbowTicks(target.elbowRad));

    telemetry.addData("shoulderDeg", Math.toDegrees(target.shoulderRad));
    telemetry.addData("elbowDeg", Math.toDegrees(target.elbowRad));
}`}</JavaCode>

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: reachable does not mean safe">

The math only knows link lengths. It does not know that your claw hits the slides, that the arm smacks
the alliance wall, or that your intake cable gets pulled tight. After the IK solver returns a valid
answer, still apply joint limits, collision rules, and current limits before commanding motors.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: singularities are fragile">

When the arm is nearly straight or nearly folded, small target changes can create large joint-angle
changes. Those configurations are called singularities. Avoid scoring paths that skim through them unless
your controller is slow, current-limited, and well tested.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="Calibrate the coordinate frame before tuning PID">

Most IK bugs are coordinate-frame bugs: the zero angle is off, the encoder sign is flipped, or inches and
ticks are mixed. Before tuning a controller, print the measured shoulder and elbow angles, move the arm by
hand, and verify that positive angles match the equations above.

</Admonition>
