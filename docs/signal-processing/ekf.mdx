# Extended Kalman Filter

<Abstract>
The Kalman filter is optimal only for linear systems, but real localisation — camera projection,
headings, and AprilTag geometry — is full of trigonometry and division by depth. This lesson extends
the filter to nonlinear models by linearising them at each step with a Jacobian, the workhorse behind
AprilTag-based pose estimation.
</Abstract>

The Kalman filter is optimal — for *linear* systems. Robot localisation is not linear: the same
AprilTag appears in different image locations and apparent sizes depending on robot position,
heading, and distance from the tag. The Extended Kalman Filter is the adaptation that makes the
Kalman machinery work on the curved models real robots actually have.

This lesson uses Kou and Haggenmiller's FTC paper,
[*Extended Kalman Filter State Estimation for Autonomous Competition Robots*](https://doi.org/10.47611/jsrhs.v12i1.5578),
as its applied anchor. They frame the FTC localisation problem as fusing three imperfect sources:
frequent odometry velocity measurements, less frequent computer-vision landmark position
measurements, and a physics-model prediction. That is exactly the reason the EKF matters in FTC:
each source is useful, but none of them is trustworthy alone.

<Problem title="Drift and nonlinear vision corrupt field coordinates">

Standard wheel-encoder odometry drifts over time — most aggressively during wheel slip and hard
turns — steadily corrupting the robot's absolute field coordinates. The natural fix is to correct it
with an absolute sensor, but AprilTag vision is not a linear measurement of pose. The camera sees a
square in the image; that square shifts, stretches, and shrinks according to the robot's position,
heading, and depth from the tag.

A plain Kalman filter assumes a fixed linear measurement matrix $H$. It cannot directly express
"if the robot turns a little, the tag moves left in the image" or "if the robot moves closer, the
tag gets larger." Worse, the nonlinearity is uneven: one pixel of image error means a small
field-position error near the tag, but a much larger error when the tag is far away.

</Problem>

<Theory title="Linearise about the current mean with a Jacobian">

The EKF keeps the Kalman predict-update heartbeat but, at each step, **linearises the nonlinear
motion and measurement models around the current mean estimate** using their *Jacobians* — matrices
of partial derivatives that give the local slope. A curve, viewed close to your current estimate, is
approximately its tangent line; on that local linearisation the ordinary Kalman update applies.

A derivative is "how fast one output changes when one input moves." A Jacobian is the same idea
when the input and output are vectors. For a simplified AprilTag camera model, let the robot state
be:

$$
\mathbf{x} =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_R \\
y_R \\
\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Here $x_R$ and $y_R$ are the robot's field position, and $\theta$ is the robot heading. Let the tag
be fixed at field position $(x_T, y_T)$. First convert the tag from field coordinates into camera
coordinates:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
X_c \\
Y_c
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & \sin\theta \\
-\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_T - x_R \\
y_T - y_R
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Here $X_c$ is forward depth in the camera frame: bigger $X_c$ means the tag is farther in front of
the camera. $Y_c$ is sideways offset in the camera frame: positive or negative depending on whether
the tag appears to one side. A simplified pinhole camera then predicts two visual measurements:

$$
h(\mathbf{x}) =
\begin{bmatrix}
u \\
s
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
f\dfrac{Y_c}{X_c} \\
f\dfrac{L}{X_c}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Here $u$ is the tag's horizontal image offset, $s$ is its apparent image size, $f$ is focal length in
pixel-like units, and $L$ is the physical tag width. This is a simplified model, not a replacement
for the full camera calibration and pose-solve used by real AprilTag pipelines, but it captures the
important EKF fact: the measurement is not a linear combination of robot pose. It divides by depth
$X_c$, so moving near a tag changes the image much more aggressively than moving far from it.

The Jacobian asks six small questions:

$$
H =
\begin{bmatrix}
\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x_R} & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial y_R} & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial \theta} \\
\dfrac{\partial s}{\partial x_R} & \dfrac{\partial s}{\partial y_R} & \dfrac{\partial s}{\partial \theta}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The first row says how the tag's image center moves when the robot pose changes. The second row
says how the tag's apparent size changes when the robot pose changes. This matters because
covariance lives in robot-state space, while camera noise lives in image-measurement space; the
Jacobian is the local converter between them.

The paper replaces the linear Kalman model with two nonlinear functions:

$$
x_{a,t} = f(x_{a,t-1}, u_t) + q_t, \qquad z_t = h(x_{a,t}) + r_t
$$

Here $x_{a,t}$ is the actual robot state at time step $t$; $u_t$ is the commanded motion or control
input; $f$ is the nonlinear state-transition function; $z_t$ is the measurement vector; $h$ maps a
state into the measurement the sensors should see; $q_t$ is process noise with covariance $Q$; and
$r_t$ is measurement noise with covariance $R$. The subscript $t-1$ means "previous loop" and $t$
means "current loop."

For the AprilTag projection model above, the cleanest way to see the Jacobian is as a chain rule.
The first matrix explains how image measurements change when camera-relative coordinates change:

$$
\frac{\partial h}{\partial (X_c,Y_c)}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
-fY_c/X_c^2 & f/X_c \\
-fL/X_c^2 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The second matrix explains how camera-relative coordinates change when the robot state changes:

$$
\frac{\partial (X_c,Y_c)}{\partial (x_R,y_R,\theta)}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
-\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & Y_c \\
\sin\theta & -\cos\theta & -X_c
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Multiplying them gives the measurement Jacobian:

$$
H =
\frac{\partial h}{\partial (X_c,Y_c)}
\frac{\partial (X_c,Y_c)}{\partial (x_R,y_R,\theta)}
$$

This is why AprilTag EKF updates feel subtle. The detector may report a simple image-space error,
but the filter must ask: at this distance and heading, how should that image error push the field
pose estimate? The Jacobian is the answer.

The EKF update then uses the *true* nonlinear $h$ for the residual while using the local Jacobian
$H$ for the covariance:

$$
H = \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\bigg|_{\hat{x}}, \qquad
\hat{x} = \hat{x}^- + K\,\big(z - h(\hat{x}^-)\big), \qquad
K = P^- H^\top (H P^- H^\top + R)^{-1}
$$

In that equation, $H$ is the measurement Jacobian, $\hat{x}^-$ is the predicted state before the
sensor update, $K$ is the Kalman gain, $P^-$ is predicted covariance, and $R$ is measurement-noise
covariance. Kou and Haggenmiller emphasize the important implementation detail: the Jacobians for
the nonlinear functions must be recalculated at each time step because the local slope changes as
the robot moves.

<Callout type="warn" title="The approximation has limits.">
Linearisation is only valid if the model is gently curved near the estimate. If the nonlinearity is
severe or the estimate is far off, the tangent misleads the filter — which is why the Unscented
Kalman Filter and particle filters exist. For typical robot localisation, the EKF is the workhorse.
The FTC paper reports the same limitation from another angle: when the loop interval $\Delta t$
gets large, Jacobian approximations and odometry accuracy both degrade.
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Where this runs on a robot">

This predict → linearise → update loop is precisely what production localisation does when it fuses
drifting odometry with intermittent AprilTag observations: odometry advances the state (predict), a
detected tag provides a nonlinear camera measurement (update), and the Jacobian maps pixel-level
uncertainty into pose-level uncertainty. In the paper's mecanum simulation, the state includes field
position, heading, and body velocity, while the measurement function combines landmark pose
information with odometry-derived encoder measurements. The matrix algebra is the same `linalg`
used in the site demos.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="EKF update structure (per loop, when a tag is seen)">{`// predict: advance pose with odometry, grow covariance P
x = motionModel(x, wheelDeltas);
P = add(mul(mul(F, P), transpose(F)), Q);     // F = motion Jacobian

// update: a tag gives nonlinear camera measurement z = h(x)
double[][] H  = aprilTagMeasurementJacobian(x, tag);  // partials of h at current x
double[][] yk = sub(z, h(x, tag));                    // residual uses true nonlinear projection
double[][] S  = add(mul(mul(H, P), transpose(H)), R);
double[][] K  = mul(mul(P, transpose(H)), inv(S));
x = add(x, mul(K, yk));
P = mul(sub(identity(n), mul(K, H)), P);`}</JavaCode>

That closes the **filter** track — turning noisy measurements into a trustworthy state estimate.
The curriculum's other half, *deciding what to do* with that estimate, is the controllers; the whole
stack is then synthesised in the capstone.

</Deploy>
