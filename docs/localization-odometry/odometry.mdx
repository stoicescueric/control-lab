# Odometry & Pose

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Odometry reconstructs the robot's field pose — position and heading — by integrating thousands of tiny
wheel movements, because no sensor reports absolute position directly. This lesson develops the
constant-curvature pose update, explains why the estimate inevitably drifts, and shows how an absolute
reference snaps it back.
</Abstract>

Autonomous routines need the robot's **pose**: position $(x, y)$ and heading $\theta$. No sensor
reports this directly, so it is reconstructed every loop from incremental motion. Doing that
reconstruction accurately — and knowing precisely how it fails — is the core of field-relative
autonomous.

<Problem title="No sensor reports absolute pose, and encoders drift">

There is no "you are here" sensor on an FTC field. Pose must be *integrated* from wheel motion, and
integration has a fundamental weakness: **errors accumulate and never cancel.** Worse, a powered
drive wheel that slips during a hard acceleration or turn reports rotation that did not translate
into ground movement, so its encoder lies. Over a match, the estimate spirals away from reality.
This is the exact limitation that motivates the filters later in the curriculum.

<Analogy>
<strong>Navigating with your eyes closed.</strong> Take ten careful steps while turning and you can
roughly track your position from memory — but small per-step errors compound, and you end up lost.
Odometry is that process, with the same failure mode.
</Analogy>

</Problem>

<Theory title="Integrating motion as a sequence of small arcs">

**Step 1 — measure the loop.** Each loop, read how far the left and right sides rolled this tick,
$d_L$ and $d_R$ (in metres). The average is how far the robot center moved forward; the difference,
divided by the track width $T$ (the distance between the two wheel tracks), is how much it turned:

$$
ds = \frac{d_L + d_R}{2}, \qquad d\theta = \frac{d_R - d_L}{T}
$$

If $d\theta \neq 0$ the robot did **not** move in a straight line — it swept a circular **arc**, and
pretending otherwise cuts the corner on every turn:

<ArcStepIllustration />

**Step 2 — the arc has a radius.** Moving forward by $ds$ while rotating by $d\theta$ is, by
definition, riding a circle whose radius is

$$
R = \frac{ds}{d\theta}
$$

A gentle turn means a huge radius; a sharp turn means a tiny one. Driving perfectly straight is just
$R = \infty$.

**Step 3 — solve the step in the robot's own frame.** Put the start of the step at the origin with the
robot facing along its local $+x$ axis. Sweeping through angle $d\theta$ on a circle of radius $R$
lands the robot at

$$
\Delta x_\text{local} = R\sin d\theta,
\qquad
\Delta y_\text{local} = R\,(1 - \cos d\theta)
$$

<ArcGeometryIllustration />

Now substitute $R = ds/d\theta$ to write both moves using only the measured $ds$ and $d\theta$:

$$
\Delta x_\text{local} = ds\,\underbrace{\frac{\sin d\theta}{d\theta}}_{\text{sinC}},
\qquad
\Delta y_\text{local} = ds\,\underbrace{\frac{1 - \cos d\theta}{d\theta}}_{\text{cosC}}
$$

Those two factors are the *only* difference between the exact arc and a naive straight step — they are
exactly what the code below calls `sinC` and `cosC`.

**Step 4 — rotate the step into the field.** The local move is relative to the robot's current heading
$\theta$, so rotate it by $\theta$ and add it to the running pose:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x &\mathrel{+}= \Delta x_\text{local}\cos\theta - \Delta y_\text{local}\sin\theta \\
y &\mathrel{+}= \Delta x_\text{local}\sin\theta + \Delta y_\text{local}\cos\theta \\
\theta &\mathrel{+}= d\theta
\end{aligned}
$$

**The straight-line limit (and why the code branches).** As $d\theta \to 0$, the factors approach
$\frac{\sin d\theta}{d\theta} \to 1$ and $\frac{1 - \cos d\theta}{d\theta} \to 0$, so the whole update
collapses to $x \mathrel{+}= ds\cos\theta$, $y \mathrel{+}= ds\sin\theta$ — the plain "just go straight"
formula. That is both a sanity check and the catch: near $d\theta = 0$ those factors are $0/0$
numerically, so the code substitutes their leading-order forms ($\frac{\sin d\theta}{d\theta} \approx 1$,
$\frac{1 - \cos d\theta}{d\theta} \approx \tfrac{1}{2}d\theta$) instead of dividing:

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Constant-curvature pose update (the algorithm)">{`double dL = leftPos  - lastLeft;       // metres rolled this loop
double dR = rightPos - lastRight;
lastLeft = leftPos;  lastRight = rightPos;

double ds  = (dL + dR) / 2.0;
double dth = (dR - dL) / TRACK_WIDTH;

double sinC, cosC;
if (Math.abs(dth) < 1e-9) {             // nearly straight -> avoid divide-by-zero
    sinC = 1.0;            cosC = 0.5 * dth;
} else {
    sinC = Math.sin(dth) / dth;
    cosC = (1 - Math.cos(dth)) / dth;
}
x       += ds * (sinC * Math.cos(heading) - cosC * Math.sin(heading));
y       += ds * (sinC * Math.sin(heading) + cosC * Math.cos(heading));
heading += dth;`}</JavaCode>

#### This is the pose exponential

What we just derived has a name you will meet all over the FTC libraries: the **pose exponential**, the
exact $SE(2)$ integral of one loop's body motion. Stacking the step into a body **twist**
$\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\Delta x_b, \Delta y_b, d\theta)$, the update is the matrix exponential

$$
\mathbf{T}_{k+1} = \mathbf{T}_k \,\exp\!\big(\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\wedge}\big),
$$

whose closed form produces *exactly* the $\frac{\sin d\theta}{d\theta}$ and $\frac{1-\cos d\theta}{d\theta}$
factors above. Road Runner's `Pose2d.exp(...)`, WPILib's `Pose2d.exp(Twist2d)`, and Pedro Pathing's
localizers all implement it — and it is strictly more general than this scalar arc formula, since a
strafing mecanum robot ($\Delta y_b \neq 0$) rides through the same map.

<Callout type="idea" title="A whole lesson on this map.">
The full $SE(2)$ derivation — the left-Jacobian $\mathbf{V}(d\theta)$, the inverse `log` map, and an
Euler-vs-exact integration demo — lives in the dedicated [Pose Exponential](./pose-exponential.mdx)
lesson. It also covers when the simple straight-line update is good enough (fast loop, tiny $d\theta$)
and when the exact map earns its keep.
</Callout>

Two engineering choices sharpen this in practice. **Heading from the IMU:** encoder-derived
$d\theta$ is corrupted by wheel scrub, so most teams take heading from the gyro and use encoders
only for distance. **Dead wheels:** unpowered omni "odometry pods" on light springs measure true
ground motion, immune to drive slip — two parallel pods give forward distance and heading, a third
(perpendicular) captures strafe.

#### From displacement back to an angle

The pose update *accumulates* heading by adding up each $d\theta$. The reverse operation — recovering an
angle from a known $(\Delta x, \Delta y)$ displacement — comes up constantly: the field-relative bearing
the robot actually traveled, or the heading it must face to drive at a target on the next page. That is
the job of the two-argument arctangent, `atan2`:

$$
\theta = \operatorname{atan2}(\Delta y, \Delta x)
$$

<Atan2Illustration />

Plain $\arctan(\Delta y / \Delta x)$ throws away the individual signs of $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$, so it
collapses opposite directions onto the same answer and only ever returns a half-circle. `atan2` keeps both
signs, so it resolves the correct quadrant and returns the full $-\pi$ to $\pi$ range — which is exactly
why every heading calculation in robotics uses it.

<Callout type="warn" title="Headings wrap; differences don't, unless you wrap them.">
Angles live on a circle, so <code>179°</code> and <code>-181°</code> are the same heading. When you
subtract a target heading from the current one, normalize the result back into <code>[-π, π]</code>
(<code>atan2(sin(e), cos(e))</code> is the clean trick) or a controller will spin the long way around a
seam that does not really exist.
</Callout>

The demo runs the algorithm live. The <span style={{ color: "#5ce08a" }}>green</span> robot is
ground truth (a perfect circle); the <span style={{ color: "#ffc24d" }}>amber</span> robot is the
odometry estimate built from imperfect encoder deltas. Raise the slip and watch it diverge — then
apply an **AprilTag fix** to snap the estimate back to truth.

<OdometryField />

<Callout type="warn" title="Odometry always decays.">
Errors only accumulate. The remedy is periodic correction from an <em>absolute</em> reference — an
AprilTag, a wall-facing distance sensor, field markings. Fusing that correction <em>optimally</em>
with the drifting estimate is exactly what a <strong>Kalman filter</strong> does.
</Callout>

<Deploy title="Read the maintained pose; reset from vision">

In practice a library maintains the pose from dead-wheel pods every loop. Your responsibilities are
tuning its constants (ticks-per-rev, wheel radius, pod offsets) and resetting from an AprilTag when
one is visible.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Road Runner pose, corrected by AprilTag">{`drive.updatePoseEstimate();              // integrate one loop of motion
Pose2d pose = drive.localizer.getPose();

double x = pose.position.x;              // configured units (e.g. inches)
double y = pose.position.y;
double heading = pose.heading.toDouble();

// When the camera resolves a tag, trust it and erase the accumulated drift:
if (tag != null) drive.localizer.setPose(tag.fieldPose);`}</JavaCode>

With a reliable pose, the final piece of autonomous is
[**following a planned path**](../path-following/pure-pursuit.mdx) — the subject of the next page.

</Deploy>
