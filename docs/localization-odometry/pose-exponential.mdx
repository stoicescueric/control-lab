# Pose Exponential

<Difficulty level="Hard" />

<Abstract>
A robot that drives and turns at the same time follows a curved arc within a single loop, not a straight
line. The pose exponential is the closed-form map that integrates that arc exactly: it takes the motion
the robot felt in its own frame (a twist) and returns the precise change in field pose. It is the
$SE(2)$ matrix exponential, and it is what Road Runner, WPILib, and Pedro Pathing all call under the
hood — recognizing it lets you read and trust their localizers instead of treating them as magic.
</Abstract>

You met this idea in passing while building [odometry](./odometry.mdx): each loop is a small
constant-curvature arc. This lesson gives that arc its proper name and its general matrix form, so you
can integrate a *strafing, turning* mecanum robot exactly — not just a tank drive going forward.

<Problem title="Straight-line integration quietly cuts every corner">

The obvious way to integrate a pose is **Euler's method**: assume the robot went straight along its
current heading for the whole loop, then apply the turn at the end.

$$
x \mathrel{+}= v\,\Delta t\,\cos\theta, \qquad
y \mathrel{+}= v\,\Delta t\,\sin\theta, \qquad
\theta \mathrel{+}= \omega\,\Delta t
$$

But if the robot is *also turning* during that loop, it actually traced a curved arc — and the straight
step chords across it, landing short and to the inside. One loop's error is tiny. Thousands of loops over
a turning autonomous path, and the corner-cutting compounds into centimetres of drift right where you can
least afford it. We want the *exact* integral of "move and turn at the same time," in closed form, cheap
enough to run every loop.

</Problem>

<Theory title="Integrate the motion as a group exponential">

### The twist: the robot's motion in its own frame

Bundle what the robot felt this loop into one body-frame vector, the **twist**:

$$
\boldsymbol{\xi} =
\begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_b \\ \Delta y_b \\ \Delta\theta \end{bmatrix}
$$

$\Delta x_b$ is forward motion, $\Delta y_b$ is sideways (strafe) motion, $\Delta\theta$ is the turn —
all *as measured in the robot's frame at the start of the loop*. A non-slipping tank step has
$\Delta y_b = 0$; a mecanum robot strafing through a turn does not. The twist is the constant velocity we
pretend the robot held for the whole step.

### A pose is a matrix, and integrating a twist is its exponential

A planar pose $(x, y, \theta)$ is an element of the group $SE(2)$ — write it as a $3\times3$ matrix that
*is* "rotate by $\theta$, then translate by $(x, y)$":

$$
\mathbf{T} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & x \\
\sin\theta & \phantom{-}\cos\theta & y \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The twist lives in the matching Lie algebra $\mathfrak{se}(2)$. The **hat** operator $(\cdot)^{\wedge}$
arranges it as the matrix whose exponential advances the pose:

$$
\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\wedge} =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -\Delta\theta & \Delta x_b \\
\Delta\theta & 0 & \Delta y_b \\
0 & 0 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Then one exact loop update is a matrix multiply by the **matrix exponential** of that twist:

$$
\mathbf{T}_{k+1} = \mathbf{T}_k \,\exp\!\big(\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\wedge}\big)
$$

That is the pose exponential. The name is literal: it is $e^{(\cdot)}$ of a matrix.

### The closed form — no series needed

You never sum the matrix series; $SE(2)$ has a tidy closed form. The rotation block is just a rotation by
$\Delta\theta$, and the translation block is the body motion passed through the **left-Jacobian**
$\mathbf{V}(\Delta\theta)$:

$$
\exp\!\big(\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\wedge}\big) =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\Delta\theta & -\sin\Delta\theta & \;\Delta x_\text{local} \\
\sin\Delta\theta & \phantom{-}\cos\Delta\theta & \;\Delta y_\text{local} \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_\text{local} \\ \Delta y_\text{local} \end{bmatrix}
= \mathbf{V}(\Delta\theta)
\begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_b \\ \Delta y_b \end{bmatrix}
$$

$$
\mathbf{V}(\Delta\theta) = \frac{1}{\Delta\theta}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sin\Delta\theta & -(1 - \cos\Delta\theta) \\[2pt]
1 - \cos\Delta\theta & \sin\Delta\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Those $\frac{\sin\Delta\theta}{\Delta\theta}$ and $\frac{1-\cos\Delta\theta}{\Delta\theta}$ factors are
*exactly* the arc terms from the [odometry](./odometry.mdx) derivation — the pose exponential is the same
constant-curvature arc, now written so a nonzero $\Delta y_b$ (strafe) rides through the very same
$\mathbf{V}$. The forward-only scalar formula cannot represent that; this can.

### The small-angle limit recovers Euler

As $\Delta\theta \to 0$, $\frac{\sin\Delta\theta}{\Delta\theta} \to 1$ and
$\frac{1-\cos\Delta\theta}{\Delta\theta} \to 0$, so $\mathbf{V} \to \mathbf{I}$ and the update collapses
back to the straight-line step. That is *why* Euler is fine at high loop rates and tight $\Delta\theta$,
and wrong when the per-loop turn is large.

### See the gap

The robot below holds one **constant twist**, so its true path is a perfect circle. The pose exponential
lands exactly on that circle every step; Euler steps straight and rotates, chording across each arc and
spiralling outward. Crank "turn per step" up (coarse, low-loop-rate integration) and watch the endpoint
error balloon; bring it down and the two converge — the small-angle limit, live.

### Going backwards: the log map

The inverse is just as useful. Given two poses, the **log map** recovers the single body twist that
carries one to the other — "what constant motion would take the robot from A to B?":

$$
\boldsymbol{\xi} = \log\!\big(\mathbf{T}_A^{-1}\,\mathbf{T}_B\big)^{\vee}
$$

That is how a path follower turns a *relative pose error* into a body-frame command, and how trajectory
libraries express a move between waypoints. `exp` integrates motion into a pose; `log` differentiates a
pose change back into motion.

</Theory>

<Deploy title="exp and log on the Control Hub">

The whole update is the left-Jacobian applied to the body twist, then composed onto the current pose —
the same `Pose2d.exp(Twist2d)` you will find in WPILib and Road Runner, written out:

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="SE(2) pose exponential and its inverse">{`/** Body-frame motion over one loop: forward, strafe (left +), and heading change. */
record Twist2d(double dx, double dy, double dtheta) {}

/** Apply a body twist to this pose exactly (the SE(2) exponential map). */
public Pose2d exp(Twist2d t) {
    double dth = t.dtheta();
    // left-Jacobian terms; fall back to the Taylor limit when the turn is tiny
    double sinc, cosc;
    if (Math.abs(dth) < 1e-9) {
        sinc = 1.0 - dth * dth / 6.0;          // sin(dth)/dth
        cosc = dth / 2.0;                        // (1 - cos(dth))/dth
    } else {
        sinc = Math.sin(dth) / dth;
        cosc = (1 - Math.cos(dth)) / dth;
    }
    // local displacement = V(dth) * body motion
    double lx = sinc * t.dx() - cosc * t.dy();
    double ly = cosc * t.dx() + sinc * t.dy();
    // rotate the local displacement by the current heading and add it on
    double c = Math.cos(heading), s = Math.sin(heading);
    return new Pose2d(
        x + lx * c - ly * s,
        y + lx * s + ly * c,
        heading + dth);
}

/** The inverse: the body twist that carries this pose to 'target' (the log map). */
public Twist2d log(Pose2d target) {
    double c = Math.cos(heading), s = Math.sin(heading);
    double dxw = target.x - x, dyw = target.y - y;
    double exb =  dxw * c + dyw * s;            // error in this pose's frame
    double eyb = -dxw * s + dyw * c;
    double dth = wrap(target.heading - heading);
    double half = dth / 2.0;
    double cot  = Math.abs(dth) < 1e-9 ? 1.0 : half / Math.tan(half);  // V^{-1} terms
    double dx =  cot * exb + half * eyb;
    double dy = -half * exb + cot * eyb;
    return new Twist2d(dx, dy, dth);
}`}</JavaCode>

Call `exp` every loop with the twist your encoders/IMU measured to integrate odometry exactly; call `log`
to turn a pose error into a body-frame command for a follower.

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="tip" title="When the straight-line update is good enough">

If your loop runs fast (small $\Delta\theta$ per step), Euler and the exponential agree to within encoder
noise, and the simpler code is fine. The pose exponential earns its keep at **low loop rates, high turn
rates, and over long autonomous paths** — exactly where corner-cutting compounds into real error.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: heading still comes from the gyro">

The exponential integrates *whatever twist you feed it* — it cannot fix a bad $\Delta\theta$. Wheel scrub
corrupts encoder-derived heading, so take $\Delta\theta$ from the IMU (or dead-wheel geometry) and use
encoders only for $\Delta x_b, \Delta y_b$. Exact integration of a wrong twist is still wrong.

</Admonition>
