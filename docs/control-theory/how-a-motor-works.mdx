# How a Motor Works

<Difficulty level="Easy" />

<Abstract>
A motor is the only actuator on the robot, and every controller you will ever write ends by choosing a
*voltage* to send it. This lesson starts from the metal — what each part inside a DC motor does, and how
brushed and brushless motors differ — then derives the two physical effects (torque from current,
back-EMF from speed) that produce the torque–speed curve. It finishes on the real goBILDA motor curve
with a worked operating point, and on what `setPower`, velocity mode, and current control actually do.
</Abstract>

Every controller in this module — PID, feedforward, motion profiling — is a sophisticated way of
answering one question: *what voltage now?* So before any of that, you have to know what the motor **is**
and what it does with that voltage. We'll build it up from the parts, not down from an equation.

<Problem title="The mechanism that's fast empty and useless under load">

A team gears a lift for very high speed. With no load on the bench, it races to the top. Hang a game
element on it and it slows to a near stop, gets too hot to touch, and two matches later browns out (a
sudden voltage drop that resets the robot) mid-climb. Nothing is broken — the motor is doing exactly what physics requires.

The team designed against a model that doesn't exist: **"voltage sets speed, full stop."** In reality a
motor trades speed for torque, pulls more current the harder you load it, and dumps the shortfall as
heat. You can't pick a gear ratio, choose a controller, or read a current spike correctly until you
know *why*.

<Analogy>
<strong>Bicycle gears on a hill.</strong> In your highest gear you fly along on flat ground but slow to a stop
the moment the road tilts up — and your legs burn doing it. Drop to a low gear and you climb easily,
just slowly. The pedals (the motor) didn't change; the <em>load</em> did. There is no gear that is fast
<em>and</em> strong at once.
</Analogy>

</Problem>

<Theory title="Build it from the parts up">

### What's inside

Pull a brushed DC motor apart and you find six things doing one job between them: turn electrical current
into a twisting force on a shaft.

<MotorExploded />

- **Magnets (the stator).** Permanent magnets line the inside of the housing and never move — they create
  a fixed magnetic field across the middle of the motor. "Stator" = the stationary part.
- **Armature (the rotor).** An iron core wrapped in coils of copper wire, mounted on the shaft. When
  current flows through those coils inside the magnets' field, each wire feels a sideways push — and
  because the coils are arranged around the shaft, those pushes add up to a *rotation*. "Rotor" = the
  rotating part.
- **Commutator.** A copper ring split into segments, riding on the shaft. As the rotor spins it swaps
  which coil the current flows through, so the push is always in the direction that keeps it turning. A
  motor without this would just twitch to one position and stop.
- **Brushes.** Two carbon blocks, spring-loaded against the commutator, that feed current into the
  spinning rotor. They literally *brush* against the rotating segments — hence the name, and hence the
  wear.
- **Housing, bearings, shaft.** The steel can holds the magnets and closes the magnetic loop; the
  bearings let the shaft spin freely; the shaft carries the torque out to your gearbox.

Current comes in the leads → through a brush → across the commutator → into a rotor coil → the magnets'
field turns that current into force → the commutator keeps swapping coils so the force keeps pushing the
same way → the shaft spins. That's the whole machine.

### Brushed vs. brushless

The brushes-and-commutator design is clever but mechanical: carbon scraping copper wears out, sparks, and
adds friction. **Brushless** motors do the same job — current in a field makes torque — but turn the
motor inside-out and switch the current *electronically*.

<BrushedVsBrushless />

| | Brushed | Brushless |
|---|---|---|
| What spins | the **windings** (rotor) | the **magnets** (rotor) |
| What's fixed | the magnets | the windings (stator) |
| Switching ("commutation") | mechanical — brushes + commutator | electronic — a controller (ESC) energizes coils in turn |
| Trade-offs | cheap, simple to drive; brushes wear & spark | efficient, long-lived, more torque/size; needs a smart controller |

<Callout type="note" title="FTC runs brushed motors.">
The goBILDA Yellow Jacket, REV HD Hex, and friends are all <strong>brushed</strong> DC motors — the
Control Hub just sets a voltage across two leads, no fancy controller required. So the rest of this
module assumes the brushed model. (Brushless is common in FRC and on hobby drones, where the controller
lives in the speed controller.)
</Callout>

### The two effects that make it a motor

Everything the motor does comes from two facts, and it is worth saying exactly what each symbol *is*.

**1 — Torque comes from current.** Current $I$ (amps) is the rate of electric charge flowing through the
coils. Every wire carrying current inside a magnetic field feels a sideways force (the Lorentz force);
summed around the rotor, that force becomes a twisting **torque** $\tau$ (newton-metres). Double the
current, double the torque:

$$
\tau = k_t\,I
$$

$k_t$ (the **torque constant**, N·m per amp) is just the proportionality — how much twist you get per amp,
set by the magnets and the number of wire turns. **Torque is current.** That single fact is what
[current control](#controlling-the-motor) below is built on.

**2 — Speed creates a voltage that fights you.** Here is the part that surprises people: a spinning motor
is *also a generator*. **Angular velocity** $\omega$ is how fast the shaft turns, in radians per second
($\omega = \text{RPM}\times 2\pi/60$ — one full turn is $2\pi$ rad). As the coils spin through the
magnets' field they generate their own voltage (Faraday's law), and it points *against* the supply
— the **back-EMF**:

$$
V_\text{emf} = k_e\,\omega
$$

<Formula>
  torque <Teal>τ = kt·I</Teal> &nbsp;·&nbsp; back-EMF <Amber>V_emf = ke·ω</Amber>
</Formula>

$k_e$ (the **back-EMF constant**, volts per rad/s) is the generator side of the same magnets and turns —
in SI units, $k_e$ and $k_t$ are numerically the same constant. The faster the motor spins, the more of
the supply voltage its own back-EMF cancels, leaving less to push current — which is why a motor stops
speeding up.

**Putting them together (Kirchhoff's voltage law).** Walk around the circuit: the voltage $V$ you apply is
split between pushing current through the windings' resistance $R$ and fighting the back-EMF:

$$
V = \underbrace{I\,R}_{\text{across the windings}} + \underbrace{k_e\,\omega}_{\text{back-EMF}}
$$

<EquationLegend
  items={[
    {symbol: 'V', meaning: 'voltage applied to the motor terminals', unit: 'volts'},
    {symbol: 'I', meaning: 'current through the motor windings', unit: 'amps'},
    {symbol: 'R', meaning: 'electrical resistance of the windings', unit: 'ohms'},
    {symbol: 'k_e', meaning: 'back-EMF constant: how many volts the spinning motor generates per angular speed', unit: 'V*s/rad'},
    {symbol: 'omega', meaning: 'motor shaft angular speed', unit: 'rad/s'},
  ]}
/>

That one equation is the entire electrical model of the motor. Read it three ways and the whole
torque–speed story falls out.

### The torque–speed line

Solve the voltage equation for current, then turn current into torque with $\tau = k_t I$:

$$
I = \frac{V - k_e\,\omega}{R}, \qquad
\tau(\omega) = \frac{k_t}{R}\big(V - k_e\,\omega\big)
= \underbrace{\frac{k_t V}{R}}_{\text{stall torque}} - \underbrace{\frac{k_t k_e}{R}}_{\text{slope}}\,\omega
$$

<EquationLegend
  title="Torque-speed symbols"
  items={[
    {symbol: 'tau', meaning: 'motor torque, the twisting force available at the motor shaft', unit: 'N*m'},
    {symbol: 'k_t', meaning: 'torque constant: torque produced per amp of winding current', unit: 'N*m/A'},
    {symbol: 'omega', meaning: 'motor shaft angular speed; higher speed creates more back-EMF', unit: 'rad/s'},
    {symbol: 'R', meaning: 'winding resistance, which turns high current into heat', unit: 'ohms'},
  ]}
/>

A straight line: maximum torque at zero speed, zero torque at top speed. Drive it below — set the voltage
and the load, and the green dot shows where the motor actually settles (where its line meets the load).
Watch **current**, **efficiency**, and **heat** as you push toward stall.

<MotorCurve />

Read off the two ends — the only points most datasheets list:

| Operating point | Speed | Torque | Current | What's happening |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Stall** ($\omega = 0$) | 0 | max, $\tau_\text{stall} = \frac{k_t V}{R} =$ **0.19 N·m** | max, $I_\text{stall} = \frac{V}{R} =$ **11 A** | no back-EMF, so current — and heat — peak |
| **Free run** (external load $\tau_\text{load} \approx 0$) | max, $\omega_\text{free} \approx \frac{V}{k_e} =$ **5900 RPM** | load torque ≈ 0 | ≈ **0.3 A** | back-EMF nearly cancels the supply; the small current covers internal friction |

Two facts fall straight out of the algebra. **Voltage slides the line, it doesn't tilt it:** stall torque
and free speed both scale with $V$, but the slope $-k_t k_e/R$ has no $V$ in it. When your code sets power
to `0.5`, it applies ~half the voltage, sliding the motor onto a lower, *parallel* line (the faint line in
the demo is full 12 V).

**Maximum power is at half speed:** output power $P_\text{out}=\tau\omega$ is zero at both ends, so it
peaks in the middle — for this motor, **29 W at ~2950 RPM**, exactly half of 5900.

<Formula>
  out <Teal>P = τ·ω</Teal> &nbsp;·&nbsp; in <Blue>P = V·I</Blue> &nbsp;·&nbsp; heat <Rose>P = I²·R</Rose>
</Formula>

<Callout type="note" title="About the numbers.">
The demo is the real <strong>goBILDA 5000-0002-0117 (MATRIX)</strong> motor at 12 V: free speed 5900 RPM,
stall torque 0.19 N·m, stall current 11 A, free current ≈ 0.3 A, peak 29 W. From those three published
numbers you get the constants: $R = 12/11 = 1.09\,\Omega$, $k_t = 0.19/11 = 0.0173$ N·m/A, and
$k_e = 12/(5900\cdot 2\pi/60) = 0.0194$ V·s/rad. These are endpoint estimates: free current and
internal friction mean $k_t$ and $k_e$ will not match perfectly even though, in ideal SI motor units,
they describe the same electromechanical coupling. (Data:
<a href="https://gm0.org/en/latest/docs/power-and-electronics/motor-guide/motor-power.html">gm0's motor guide</a>.)
</Callout>

### A worked example

Say this motor drives a mechanism at 12 V against a steady **0.05 N·m** load. Where does it settle?

$$
\omega = \frac{V - \tau R / k_t}{k_e}
= \frac{12 - (0.05)(1.09)/0.0173}{0.0194}
\approx 455\ \text{rad/s} = \mathbf{4350\ RPM}
$$

The current it pulls, and the power bookkeeping:

$$
I = \frac{V - k_e\omega}{R} = \frac{12 - (0.0194)(455)}{1.09} \approx \mathbf{2.9\ A}
$$

$$
P_\text{out} = \tau\omega \approx \mathbf{22.8\ W}, \quad
P_\text{in} = VI \approx 34.7\ \text{W}, \quad
\eta = \frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}} \approx \mathbf{66\%}, \quad
P_\text{heat} = I^2 R \approx 9\ \text{W}
$$

A healthy operating point: fast, efficient, modest current. Now push the load to stall (0.19 N·m): speed
→ 0, current → 11 A, output power → 0, and **all** 130 W of input becomes heat. That is the lift that
"works empty." Set the demo's load slider to 0.05 and then to 0.19 and watch these exact numbers.

### Controlling the motor

You never command torque or current directly — you command **voltage** (the Control Hub does it by PWM:
switching 12 V on and off fast, so `setPower(0.5)` delivers ~6 V on average). Three levels of control build
on the model above:

1. **Open loop — `setPower`.** You pick a voltage and live with wherever the load puts you on the line.
   Simple, but speed sags with load and the battery.
2. **Velocity feedback — `RUN_USING_ENCODER`.** A loop measures speed and nudges the voltage to hold a
   target as the load changes — it slides the operating point along the curve for you. It still can't beat
   the curve's limits: never faster than free speed, never stronger than stall.
3. **Torque / current control.** Because **torque is current** ($\tau = k_t I$), commanding a current
   *is* commanding a torque. Rearranging the voltage equation, to drive a chosen current $I^*$ at the
   current speed $\omega$ you apply:

$$
V_\text{cmd} = k_e\,\omega + I^*\,R
$$

   You read $\omega$ from the encoder, predict the back-EMF, add the bit of voltage that pushes your
   target current, and send it. Be precise about what this is: it is **open-loop, model-based** current
   control — a current *feedforward*. It trusts the constants $k_e, k_t, R$ and never measures the actual
   current, so any error in the model (a warm motor's higher $R$, a sagging battery) shows up directly as
   current error.

   **True closed-loop current control** instead measures the current each loop (`getCurrent()`) and runs
   a feedback controller on the error — what a dedicated motor controller or FOC drive does internally.
   FTC's hub exposes no fast current loop to command, so the model-based version above is the practical
   one, optionally backstopped by the *measured*-current limiter in the Deploy section below.

   **Current-limiting** this way is how good drives stop wheel slip (cap the torque the tires can use),
   avoid overheating motors, and survive defense. In a pushing match the wheels are stalled ($\omega = 0$),
   so a raw full-power command pulls ~$V/R$ — tens of amps — and trips the breaker; a current limit instead
   drops the applied voltage to whatever delivers the safe current.

<Callout type="idea" title="This is the whole module in one equation.">
$V = k_e\omega + IR$ is the seed of everything ahead. <strong>Feedforward</strong> is this equation solved
for the voltage a target speed needs ($k_S, k_V, k_A$ are its terms). <strong>System identification</strong>
measures $k_e, k_t, R$ for your real motor. <strong>Current control</strong> (model-based here, closed-loop
on a real controller) is it solved for a target torque. Learn the motor and the controllers stop being magic.
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Gearing, current, and what your code commands">

You bolt a gearbox to the motor to *move the useful part of the curve* to where your mechanism lives. A
reduction $G$ divides output speed by $G$ and multiplies output torque by $G$ (minus losses):

<Formula>
  output speed = <Blue>ω_free / G</Blue> &nbsp;·&nbsp; output torque = <Rose>τ_stall · G · η</Rose>
</Formula>

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Motor specs -> geared output (use your datasheet numbers)">{`/** The three numbers every brushed DC motor publishes at 12 V. */
public record MotorSpec(
        double freeSpeedRpm,    // RPM at no load
        double stallTorqueNm,   // N·m at zero speed
        double stallCurrentA) { // A at zero speed (the heat budget)

    public double gearedFreeSpeedRpm(double gearReduction) {
        return freeSpeedRpm / gearReduction;             // speed divides down
    }
    public double gearedStallTorqueNm(double gearReduction, double efficiency) {
        return stallTorqueNm * gearReduction * efficiency; // torque multiplies up
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

At runtime, a motor near stall is the fastest way to overheat a mechanism and brown out the robot. The hub can
read current, so guard it — or, better, command current directly using the model.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Feedforward (model-based) current control, and a measured-current limiter">{`/** FEEDFORWARD / model-based (OPEN-LOOP) current control: command the voltage the
 *  model predicts will yield targetAmps, via V = ke*omega + I*R, then voltage-
 *  compensate. It never reads the actual current, so it is only as good as ke/R.
 *  For a TRUE closed loop, run a controller on the getCurrent() error instead. */
public void setCurrent(DcMotorEx motor, double targetAmps) {
    double omega = motor.getVelocity(AngleUnit.RADIANS);  // rad/s from the encoder
    double vCmd  = KE * omega + targetAmps * R;           // predicted back-EMF + IR
    motor.setPower(vCmd / battery.getVoltage());          // volts -> [-1, 1]
}

/** Closed-loop safety net: MEASURE the current and scale the command down if it
 *  exceeds the budget. This is feedback (it reads getCurrent), unlike setCurrent. */
public void driveSafely(DcMotorEx motor, double command, double maxCurrentA) {
    double current = motor.getCurrent(CurrentUnit.AMPS);
    if (current > maxCurrentA) command *= maxCurrentA / current;
    motor.setPower(command);
}`}</JavaCode>

<Callout type="warn" title="Stall is a thermal event, not just a slow one.">
A motor held near stall draws stall current <em>continuously</em>, and almost all of it becomes heat
($I^2R$) — enough to demagnetize or melt the windings and to drag the battery down until the hub browns
out. Never gear a mechanism so its normal load sits near stall, and never hold a pushing match at full
power. Current control, or a current cap, is the fix.
</Callout>

The design move: take your motor's three datasheet numbers, pick $G$ so the mechanism's real load lands in
the fast, efficient stretch of the line — not crammed against stall — and current-limit so a jam can't
exceed the heat budget. Next, [**Feedforward**](./feedforward.mdx) turns this same model around to ask:
given the speed I *want*, what voltage do I send before any error even appears?

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="warning" title="The ideal model, and where it bends">

Real motors add friction (so free current isn't quite zero and free speed is a touch lower), winding
inductance, and gearbox losses — the perfect straight line is slightly curved. It is still accurate enough
to design with. Measuring the *real* constants for your specific motor is its own lesson:
[system identification](./system-identification.mdx).

</Admonition>
