# How a Motor Works

<Abstract>
A DC motor converts electrical voltage into motion through two coupled effects — the torque it makes
is proportional to the current it draws, and its own spinning generates a "back-EMF" voltage that
grows with speed — which together produce a straight-line trade-off between how fast it spins and how
hard it can push. This lesson builds that torque–speed curve from first principles and uses it to
explain why a motor slows under load, spikes its current near stall, and turns the shortfall into
heat.
</Abstract>

A motor is the only actuator on the robot — every controller in this module, in the end, just
chooses a *voltage* to send it. PID, feedforward, motion profiling: all of them are sophisticated
ways of answering one question, "what voltage now?" So before any of that, you have to know what the
motor *does* with that voltage. And the honest answer is more interesting than "more volts, more
speed."

<Problem title="The mechanism that's fast empty and useless under load">

A team gears a lift for blistering speed. On the bench, unloaded, it screams to the top. Then they
hang a game element on it and it bogs to a crawl, the motor gets too hot to touch, and two matches
later it browns out the whole robot mid-climb. Nothing is "broken." The motor is behaving exactly as
physics requires — the team just designed against a mental model that doesn't exist.

That wrong model is **"voltage sets speed, full stop."** In reality a motor trades speed for torque:
the harder you load it, the slower it turns, the more current it pulls, and the more of your battery
ends up as heat instead of motion. You cannot pick a gear ratio, choose a controller, or read a
current spike correctly until that tradeoff is in your bones.

<Analogy>
<strong>Bicycle gears on a hill.</strong> In your highest gear you fly on the flat but grind to a
dead stop the instant the road tilts up — and your legs burn doing it. Drop to a low gear and you
climb easily, just slowly. The pedals (the motor) didn't change; the <em>load</em> did, and there is
no gear that is fast <em>and</em> strong at once. A motor lives on exactly this tradeoff curve.
</Analogy>

</Problem>

Here is why the line looks like that. A DC motor obeys two simple proportionalities:

- **Torque comes from current.** The magnetic force on the windings is proportional to the current
  through them: $\tau = k_t\, I$.
- **Speed creates a voltage that fights you.** A spinning motor is also a generator. It produces a
  *back-EMF* proportional to its speed, $V_\text{emf} = k_e\,\omega$, pushing back against the supply.

<Formula>
  torque <Teal>τ = kt·I</Teal> &nbsp;·&nbsp; back-EMF <Amber>V_emf = ke·ω</Amber>
</Formula>

Now write Kirchhoff's voltage law around the armature. The applied voltage $V$ is split between the
resistance of the windings and that back-EMF:

$$
V = \underbrace{I\,R}_{\text{across the windings}} + \underbrace{k_e\,\omega}_{\text{back-EMF}}
$$

Solve for current, then multiply by $k_t$ to get torque as a function of speed:

$$
I = \frac{V - k_e\,\omega}{R}, \qquad
\tau(\omega) = \frac{k_t}{R}\,\big(V - k_e\,\omega\big)
= \underbrace{\frac{k_t V}{R}}_{\text{stall torque}} - \underbrace{\frac{k_t k_e}{R}}_{\text{slope}}\,\omega
$$

That is the straight line in the demo. Read off its two ends — the only two operating points most
datasheets bother to list:

| Operating point | Speed | Torque | Current | What's happening |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Stall** ($\omega = 0$) | none | max, $\tau_\text{stall} = \frac{k_t V}{R}$ | max, $I_\text{stall} = \frac{V}{R}$ | no back-EMF, so current — and heat — peak |
| **Free run** ($\tau = 0$) | max, $\omega_\text{free} = \frac{V}{k_e}$ | none | ≈ 0 | back-EMF nearly cancels the supply |

Two consequences fall straight out of the algebra, and both are worth internalizing:

**Voltage slides the line; it doesn't tilt it.** Stall torque and free speed *both* scale with $V$,
but the slope $-k_t k_e / R$ has no $V$ in it. Halve the voltage and the whole line moves inward,
parallel to itself (the faint line in the demo is full 12 V for comparison). When your code sets motor
power to `0.5`, *this* is what it does — it applies roughly half the voltage, sliding the motor onto a
lower-but-parallel line.

**Maximum power is at half speed.** Output power is $P_\text{out} = \tau\,\omega$ — zero at stall (no
motion) and zero at free run (no force), so it peaks in the middle, at exactly
$\omega_\text{free}/2$. Meanwhile the electrical input is $P_\text{in} = V I$, and the difference is
dumped as heat, $P_\text{heat} = I^2 R$. Near stall the current is enormous and the motion is nearly
zero, so almost all of that power becomes heat — efficiency collapses.

<Formula>
  out <Teal>P = τ·ω</Teal> &nbsp;·&nbsp; in <Blue>P = V·I</Blue> &nbsp;·&nbsp; heat <Rose>P = I²·R</Rose>
</Formula>

<Callout type="idea" title="Three things to try in the demo:">
<ol>
<li><strong>Free run</strong> — load at zero. Top speed, almost no current, but zero useful work and
0% efficiency. Speed alone does nothing.</li>
<li><strong>A healthy load</strong> — the default. The dot sits in the fast, efficient stretch near
the top of the line; current is modest.</li>
<li><strong>Stall it</strong> — load past the stall torque. The motor can't move it, speed pins to
zero, current jumps to its maximum, and the heat readout goes through the roof. This is the lift that
"works empty."</li>
</ol>
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Gearing, current, and what your code actually controls">

You almost never run a bare motor — you bolt a gearbox to it, and the gearbox is how you *move the
useful part of the curve* to where your mechanism lives. A reduction of $G$ divides the output speed
by $G$ and multiplies the output torque by $G$ (minus gearbox losses):

<Formula>
  output speed = <Blue>ω_free / G</Blue> &nbsp;·&nbsp; output torque = <Rose>τ_stall · G · η</Rose>
</Formula>

So the design move is: take your motor's three datasheet numbers, pick $G$ so the mechanism's real
load lands in the fast, efficient part of the line — not crammed against stall. The model is small
enough to keep in code:

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Motor specs → geared output (fill in your datasheet numbers)">{`/**
 * The three numbers every DC motor publishes at nominal (12 V) voltage.
 * Fill these in from YOUR motor's datasheet — nothing here is assumed for you.
 */
public record MotorSpec(
        double freeSpeedRpm,    // RPM at no load
        double stallTorqueNm,   // N·m at zero speed
        double stallCurrentA) { // A at zero speed  (this is the heat budget)

    /** Output free speed after a reduction gearbox: speed divides down. */
    public double gearedFreeSpeedRpm(double gearReduction) {
        return freeSpeedRpm / gearReduction;
    }

    /** Output stall torque after a reduction: torque multiplies up, minus losses. */
    public double gearedStallTorqueNm(double gearReduction, double efficiency) {
        return stallTorqueNm * gearReduction * efficiency;
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

The other half is at runtime: a motor sitting near stall is the single most common way to cook a
mechanism and brown out a robot. The Control Hub can read motor current directly, so guard against it.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Back off before a stalled motor cooks itself">{`/**
 * Near stall the motor pulls its full stall current and turns almost all of it
 * into heat (I²R), sagging the battery and tripping the hub's protection. If the
 * current runs away, scale the command back into a safe budget.
 */
public void driveSafely(DcMotorEx motor, double command, double maxCurrentA) {
    double current = motor.getCurrent(CurrentUnit.AMPS);
    if (current > maxCurrentA) {
        command *= maxCurrentA / current;   // throttle to the current budget
    }
    motor.setPower(command);
}`}</JavaCode>

<Callout type="warn" title="Stall is a thermal event, not just a slow one.">
A motor held near stall draws stall current <em>continuously</em>. That energy has nowhere to go but
into the windings as heat — enough to demagnetize or melt them, and enough to drag the battery
voltage down until the Control Hub browns out. Never gear a mechanism so its normal load sits near
stall, and never hold a pushing match at full power.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="This is why RUN_USING_ENCODER exists.">
The SDK's velocity mode runs a feedback loop that adjusts voltage to hold a target speed as the load
changes — it's nudging the operating point along the curve for you. Knowing the curve tells you its
hard limits: it can never exceed the free speed, and under heavy load it must give up speed or pull
huge current. No control loop repeals the physics.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note" title="The ideal model, and where it bends.">
Real motors add friction (so free current isn't quite zero and free speed is a touch lower),
winding inductance, and gearbox losses — the perfect straight line is slightly curved. It's still
accurate enough to design with. Measuring the <em>real</em> $k_t$, $k_e$, and $R$ for your specific
motor is a later lesson: system identification.
</Callout>

Notice what those three constants are doing. The current term $k_t/R$ that overcomes friction, the
back-EMF term $k_e \omega$ that grows with speed, the resistance that fights acceleration — these are
*exactly* the quantities the next lesson packages into the feedforward gains
$k_S$, $k_V$, and $k_A$. Feedforward is this motor model, turned around to ask: given the speed I
*want*, what voltage should I send before any error even appears? On to
[**Feedforward**](./feedforward.mdx).
