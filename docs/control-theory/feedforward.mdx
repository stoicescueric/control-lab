# Feedforward (kS, kV, kA, kG)

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Feedforward is the predictive half of a controller: instead of waiting for an error to appear, it uses
a model of the mechanism to command most of the required effort up front, leaving feedback to mop up
the rest. This lesson derives the kS, kV, and kA terms straight from the motor's physics and combines
them with PID, then extends the model to gravity-loaded FTC mechanisms: slides/elevators and arms.
</Abstract>

Feedback is reactive by construction. Feedforward is the predictive half: a model of the mechanism
that supplies most of the required command immediately, leaving feedback to correct only the small
residual. It is the difference between a sluggish velocity loop and a crisp one.

<Problem title="A reactive controller must first fail to respond">

A pure feedback controller does nothing until an error exists, then scrambles to close it. On a
velocity-controlled mechanism — a flywheel, a drivetrain at cruise — this guarantees lag and droop:
the controller can only hold a speed by *maintaining* the error that produces the command. You can
mask it with a large gain, but high feedback gain makes the system twitchy and prone to overshoot.
The information to do better already exists: you *know* roughly what voltage a given speed requires.

<Analogy>
<strong>Cresting a hill.</strong> A skilled driver presses the accelerator <em>as</em> the hill
begins, anticipating the load. A reactive driver waits until the car has already slowed, then
over-corrects. Feedforward is the anticipation; feedback only cleans up the difference.
</Analogy>

</Problem>

<Theory title="A physical model of the required voltage">

To hold a target velocity $v$ (and optionally accelerate at $a$), the predicted voltage is:

$$
V = \underbrace{k_S\,\operatorname{sign}(v)}_{\text{break friction}}
  + \underbrace{k_V\,v}_{\text{hold the speed}}
  + \underbrace{k_A\,a}_{\text{accelerate}}
$$

<Formula>
  V = <Amber>kS·sign(v)</Amber> + <Teal>kV·v</Teal> + <Blue>kA·a</Blue>
</Formula>

These constants are not fudge factors — they are the [motor's own physics](./how-a-motor-works.mdx).
A DC motor obeys $V = IR + k_e\omega$: the back-EMF term $k_e\omega$ rises with speed (that is
$k_V v$), while the
current term supplies torque to overcome static friction (a constant, $k_S$) and to accelerate the
inertia, $\tau = J\alpha$ (that is $k_A a$).

| Term | Counteracts | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **kS** — static | friction / stiction | the minimum voltage to start motion |
| **kV** — velocity | back-EMF / drag | volts per unit speed — the bulk of the command |
| **kA** — acceleration | inertia (mass) | extra volts to change speed; only while the target moves |

## Mechanism feedforward: add gravity

The flywheel model is missing one thing many FTC mechanisms care about: **weight**. A flywheel does not
need voltage to hold still against gravity. A vertical slide does. An arm definitely does, and the amount
changes with angle.

<MechanismFeedforward />

### Slides and elevators: gravity is mostly constant

For a vertical slide, define:

- $h$ as slide height.
- $v = \dot h$ as slide velocity, positive upward.
- $a = \ddot h$ as slide acceleration.
- $k_G$ as the voltage needed to hold the slide still against gravity.

The ideal vertical-slide feedforward is:

$$
\boxed{
V_{slide} =
k_S\,\operatorname{sign}(v)
+ k_G
+ k_V\,v
+ k_A\,a
}
$$

The important detail is that $k_G$ does **not** flip sign when the slide moves downward. Static friction
depends on direction, so $k_S\operatorname{sign}(v)$ changes. Gravity still pulls down, so the motor still
needs an upward holding term.

Real FTC slides are not always ideal elevators. String wrap diameter, cable routing, elastic tension,
carriage friction, arm coupling, or changing extension geometry can make the required hold voltage drift
with height. When one constant $k_G$ is not enough, measure the hold voltage at several heights and
interpolate:

$$
V_{slide} =
k_S\,\operatorname{sign}(v)
+ k_G(h)
+ k_V\,v
+ k_A\,a
$$

Between two calibration points $(h_i, k_{G,i})$ and $(h_{i+1}, k_{G,i+1})$:

$$
\boxed{
k_G(h)
=
k_{G,i}
+
\frac{h - h_i}{h_{i+1} - h_i}
\left(k_{G,i+1} - k_{G,i}\right)
}
$$

where $h$ is the current or target slide height, $h_i$ and $h_{i+1}$ are the two measured heights around
it, and $k_{G,i}$ / $k_{G,i+1}$ are the measured hold voltages at those heights.

### Arms: gravity changes with angle

For an arm, define:

- $\theta$ as the arm angle measured from **horizontal**.
- $\omega = \dot\theta$ as angular velocity.
- $\alpha = \ddot\theta$ as angular acceleration.
- $k_G$ as the voltage needed to hold the arm when it is horizontal.

Gravity torque is largest when the arm is horizontal and nearly zero when the arm is vertical. That gives
the arm feedforward:

$$
\boxed{
V_{arm} =
k_S\,\operatorname{sign}(\omega)
+ k_G\cos(\theta)
+ k_V\,\omega
+ k_A\,\alpha
}
$$

This convention matters. If your encoder reads zero somewhere else, convert it first:

$$
\theta_{horizontal} = \theta_{encoder} + \theta_{offset}
$$

Then use $\cos(\theta_{horizontal})$. If the sign is wrong, do not randomly negate the whole controller;
fix the angle convention and motor direction until a horizontal arm receives the expected upward holding
voltage.

The deployable pattern is **feedforward + feedback**: the model does ~90 % of the work instantly,
[PID](./pid.mdx) corrects the residual and rejects disturbances.

<Formula>
  output = <Teal>feedforward(goal)</Teal> + <Rose>PID(measurement, goal)</Rose>
</Formula>

In the demo, the motor must reach the dashed target speed. Toggle feedforward on and off with
identical gains; the bars show how the command splits between the
<span style={{ color: "#5ce08a" }}>feedforward prediction</span> and the
<span style={{ color: "#ffc24d" }}>feedback correction</span>.

<Callout type="idea" title="Three experiments:">
<ol>
<li>Feedforward <strong>ON</strong>, tiny kP — it reaches and holds the target; the green prediction
bar carries the load.</li>
<li>Feedforward <strong>OFF</strong>, same kP — it settles below target (steady-state error).
Raising kP to compensate makes it overshoot and twitch.</li>
<li>Mistune <strong>kV</strong> low — the prediction under-shoots and feedback must cover the gap;
the main benefit is lost. Accurate constants matter.</li>
</ol>
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Volts on a battery that sags">

FTC motors take power in `-1..1`, but the model is in volts. Dividing by the *measured* battery
voltage keeps behaviour constant whether the pack is at 13 V or 11 V — see
[voltage compensation](./voltage-compensation.mdx).

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Feedforward + feedback, battery-compensated">{`public class Flywheel {
    public static double kS = 0.7, kV = 2.4, kA = 0.0, kP = 2.0;   // @Config-tunable

    private final DcMotorEx motor;
    private final VoltageSensor battery;

    public Flywheel(HardwareMap hw) {
        motor   = hw.get(DcMotorEx.class, "flywheel");
        battery = hw.voltageSensor.iterator().next();
        motor.setMode(DcMotor.RunMode.RUN_USING_ENCODER);
    }

    public void run(double goalVel, double goalAccel) {        // real units, e.g. m/s
        double measVel = motor.getVelocity() * METERS_PER_TICK;

        double predict = kS * Math.signum(goalVel) + kV * goalVel + kA * goalAccel;
        double correct = kP * (goalVel - measVel);

        motor.setPower((predict + correct) / battery.getVoltage());   // volts -> -1..1
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Arm feedforward with kG * cos(theta)">{`/**
 * Feedforward model for a single-joint arm.
 *
 * <p>Angle convention: angleRad = 0 means the arm is horizontal.
 * If your encoder zero is elsewhere, add an offset before calling calculate().</p>
 */
public final class ArmFeedforward {
    private final double kS;
    private final double kG;
    private final double kV;
    private final double kA;

    public ArmFeedforward(double kS, double kG, double kV, double kA) {
        this.kS = kS;
        this.kG = kG;
        this.kV = kV;
        this.kA = kA;
    }

    /**
     * @param angleRad arm angle from horizontal, radians
     * @param velocityRadPerSec target angular velocity
     * @param accelRadPerSecSq target angular acceleration
     * @return predicted motor voltage
     */
    public double calculate(double angleRad, double velocityRadPerSec, double accelRadPerSecSq) {
        double staticVolts = Math.abs(velocityRadPerSec) < 1e-4
            ? 0.0
            : kS * Math.signum(velocityRadPerSec);

        return staticVolts
            + kG * Math.cos(angleRad)
            + kV * velocityRadPerSec
            + kA * accelRadPerSecSq;
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Slide feedforward with interpolated gravity">{`/**
 * Feedforward model for a vertical slide/elevator.
 *
 * <p>The gravity term is interpolated from measured hold voltages at known
 * heights. Use this when one constant kG is not accurate across the full travel.</p>
 */
public final class InterpolatedSlideFeedforward {
    private final double kS;
    private final double kV;
    private final double kA;
    private final double[] heights;
    private final double[] holdVolts;

    public InterpolatedSlideFeedforward(
            double kS,
            double kV,
            double kA,
            double[] heights,
            double[] holdVolts) {
        if (heights.length != holdVolts.length || heights.length < 2) {
            throw new IllegalArgumentException("Need matching height and hold-voltage samples.");
        }
        this.kS = kS;
        this.kV = kV;
        this.kA = kA;
        this.heights = heights.clone();
        this.holdVolts = holdVolts.clone();
    }

    /**
     * @param height slide height in your chosen real unit
     * @param velocity target slide velocity, positive upward
     * @param acceleration target slide acceleration, positive upward
     * @return predicted motor voltage
     */
    public double calculate(double height, double velocity, double acceleration) {
        double staticVolts = Math.abs(velocity) < 1e-4
            ? 0.0
            : kS * Math.signum(velocity);

        return staticVolts
            + interpolateHoldVolts(height)
            + kV * velocity
            + kA * acceleration;
    }

    private double interpolateHoldVolts(double height) {
        if (height <= heights[0]) {
            return holdVolts[0];
        }

        for (int i = 0; i < heights.length - 1; i++) {
            double leftH = heights[i];
            double rightH = heights[i + 1];
            if (height <= rightH) {
                double t = (height - leftH) / (rightH - leftH);
                return holdVolts[i] + t * (holdVolts[i + 1] - holdVolts[i]);
            }
        }

        return holdVolts[holdVolts.length - 1];
    }
}`}</JavaCode>

The identical recipe in FRC is one line, because WPILib bundles it:

<JavaCode league="FRC" label="WPILib equivalent">{`SimpleMotorFeedforward ff = new SimpleMotorFeedforward(kS, kV, kA);
PIDController pid = new PIDController(kP, 0, 0);
motor.setVoltage(ff.calculate(goalVel) + pid.calculate(measVel, goalVel));`}</JavaCode>

The constants come from **measurement**, not guesswork. For velocity mechanisms, drive the mechanism
through known voltages and fit the steady speeds. For gravity mechanisms, measure the hold voltage at the
important positions: a slide at several heights, or an arm at known angles. A useful first estimate is
$k_V \approx 12\,\text{V} \div v_{\max}$, but to get them right you measure them:
[**system identification**](./system-identification.mdx) recovers $k_S$, $k_V$, and $k_A$ by
least-squares fit, exactly as FRC's SysId does.

<Callout type="warn" title="Feedforward is only as good as its units.">
<code>kV</code> in "volts per m/s" is meaningless unless <code>getVelocity()</code> is truly
converted to m/s. A wrong ticks→units factor produces a wrong model — exactly the failure
[Discretization &amp; Units](../software-architecture/discretization.mdx) guards against.
</Callout>

Feedforward is only ever half a controller, though — it is open-loop, blind to anything it didn't
model: a jammed mechanism, a worn gear, a battery having a bad day. Those gains are also only as good as
your measurement of them — so pair feedforward with [**system identification**](./system-identification.mdx)
to measure $k_S, k_V, k_A$ from data, measure $k_G$ on the mechanism itself, and use [**PID**](./pid.mdx)
for the feedback half that catches everything the model missed.

</Deploy>
