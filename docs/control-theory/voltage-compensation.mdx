# Voltage Compensation

<Difficulty level="Easy" />

<Abstract>
A motor power command in $[-1, 1]$ delivers that fraction of the *battery's current voltage*, not a fixed
voltage — so as the pack sags over a match, the same command produces less and less. Voltage compensation
divides the intended voltage by the measured battery voltage before sending it, delivering a constant
target voltage (until the battery physically can't), which keeps feedforward gains valid and behavior
repeatable from the first second to the last.
</Abstract>

This is the shortest lesson in the module and one of the most valuable: a few characters of code that
make every other controller — [feedforward](./feedforward.mdx), [PID](./pid.mdx),
[profiles](./motion-profiling.mdx) — behave the same on a fresh pack and a tired one.

<Problem title="The same command, quietly doing less">

`motor.setPower(0.6)` does **not** apply a fixed voltage. It applies 60% of whatever the battery happens
to read right now. A freshly charged pack sits near 13.5 V; after a few sprints and a climb it sags toward
11 V under load. So `0.6` is $0.6 \times 13.5 = 8.1$ V early in the match and $0.6 \times 11 = 6.6$ V
late — nearly a 20% drop in effort for the identical command. Your auto that was tuned on a full battery
now undershoots; your flywheel that was on speed now falls short. Worse, your carefully *measured*
feedforward gains are in **volts** — and they're being applied to a command that no longer maps to volts.

</Problem>

<Theory title="Divide by what you actually have">

Every controller in this module computes a desired *voltage* — feedforward's $k_S + k_V v + k_A a$, PID's
output, a profile's command. To turn a desired voltage $V_\text{cmd}$ into the power fraction that will
actually deliver it, divide by the **measured** battery voltage:

$$
\text{power} = \frac{V_\text{cmd}}{V_\text{battery}}
$$

Now the delivered voltage is $\text{power} \times V_\text{battery} = V_\text{cmd}$ — independent of the
battery, exactly what you asked for. The only thing that can break the guarantee is physics: you cannot
deliver more than the battery holds, so once $V_\text{cmd} > V_\text{battery}$ the command saturates at
full power and the output follows the sagging pack down.

In the demo, the rose line is a raw command (delivered volts $=$ power $\times$ battery, drifting as the
pack changes); the green line is the compensated command, flat at your target until the battery can no
longer reach it. Drag the battery marker from full to sagging and watch the raw output fall away while the
compensated one holds.

<Callout type="idea" title="It makes your gains portable in time.">
Because compensation pins the command to real volts, the feedforward constants you
[measured](./system-identification.mdx) stay correct all match — and even transfer better between two
robots with the same mechanism. Without it, every gain is implicitly "valid at whatever voltage I tuned
on," which is never the voltage you compete on.
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="One division, or one SDK call">

Apply it at the very end, after the controller has produced a voltage. Read the battery once per loop
(reading it is not free) and clamp the result to the legal power range.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="Voltage-compensated output">{`private final VoltageSensor battery = hardwareMap.voltageSensor.iterator().next();

/** Send a desired VOLTAGE to a motor, compensated for the live battery voltage. */
public void setVoltage(DcMotorEx motor, double desiredVolts) {
    double vbat = battery.getVoltage();              // measured this loop
    double power = desiredVolts / vbat;              // volts -> [-1, 1]
    motor.setPower(Math.max(-1.0, Math.min(1.0, power)));
}

// every controller in this module ends the same way:
//   double volts = kS*Math.signum(v) + kV*v + kA*a + pid.calculate(meas, target);
//   setVoltage(motor, volts);`}</JavaCode>

In FRC, the motor controllers do it for you with `enableVoltageCompensation(nominalVolts)` — set it once
and command in volts with `setVoltage(...)` thereafter. The idea is identical: stop thinking in
unitless "power" and start thinking in volts the battery may or may not be able to supply.

<Callout type="tip" title="Compensation is not a brownout cure.">
Holding the *command* constant as voltage drops means drawing *more current* to do it — which sags the
pack further. Compensation keeps behavior consistent within the battery's ability; it cannot create energy
that isn't there. If you're browning out, the fix is in the [motor model](./how-a-motor-works.mdx) (you're
living too near stall), not here.
</Callout>

</Deploy>

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: filter the voltage reading">

Battery voltage spikes downward sharply under sudden load (a drivetrain slamming to full power), so a raw
reading can momentarily read very low and make your compensation over-command. Run the voltage through a
light [low-pass filter](../signal-processing/low-pass.mdx), and read it once per loop rather than per
motor — the sensor call has real latency.

</Admonition>
