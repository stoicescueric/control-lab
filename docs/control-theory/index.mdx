# Module 4 · Motor Dynamics & Control Theory

This is the heart of the curriculum. A motor is a physical system with a knowable model; a controller is
the math that commands it to do what you want despite gravity, friction, and a sagging battery. We start
with the motor itself, learn to *predict* the command it needs (feedforward), then *correct* whatever is
left with feedback (PID) — and combine the two into a single output.

## What this module covers

The lessons are ordered by **difficulty**, easiest first:

1. **The DC-motor model & motor curve** *(Easy)* — torque, speed, and back-EMF; why a motor slows under
   load.
2. **Voltage compensation** *(Easy)* — holding behavior constant as the battery drops over a match.
3. **Feedforward (kS, kV, kA)** *(Medium)* — computing the command *before* the error appears.
4. **PID** *(Medium)* — the three-term feedback controller, hardened for real hardware.
5. **Bang-bang** *(Medium)* — when the simplest possible controller is the right one (flywheels); its
   take-back-half upgrade lives in [Advanced Topics](../advanced-topics/takeback-half.mdx).
6. **System identification** *(Hard)* — recovering kS/kV/kA from data by least-squares, the way SysId does.
7. **Motion profiling** *(Hard)* — feeding the controller a physically achievable trajectory instead of a
   step.

<Admonition type="warning" title="Feedback alone is always a step behind">

A pure PID controller cannot act until an error already exists. The fast, accurate mechanisms you see
at competition pair feedback with a feedforward model of the plant. Understanding the motor model is
what makes that pairing possible.

</Admonition>
