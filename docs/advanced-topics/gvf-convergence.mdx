# Guided Vector Fields — Convergence Theory

<Difficulty level="Hard" />

<Abstract>
The [guided-vector-field lesson](../path-following/guided-vector-fields.mdx) builds the field from the
closest point on the path — the form you code. This page gives the other half: the control-theory
formulation that writes the path as the zero level set of a smooth function, builds the same field from its
gradient, and <em>proves</em> convergence with a Lyapunov argument. Read the core lesson first; this is the
"why it provably works."
</Abstract>

<Admonition type="note" title="Prerequisite">

This page extends [**Guided Vector Fields**](../path-following/guided-vector-fields.mdx). It assumes you
already know the closest-point field $\boldsymbol{\chi} = \hat{\mathbf{t}} - k_N\,e\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ and
the cross-track error $e$ from that lesson.

</Admonition>

## The same field, written as a level set

The closest-point recipe is what you code, but it isn't the cleanest object to *prove* things about,
because "closest point" is itself the output of a search. The control-theory formulation (Kapitanyuk,
Proskurnikov & Cao) writes the path as the **zero level set of a smooth function** $\varphi(x,y)$ — for a
circle of radius $R$, $\varphi = x^2 + y^2 - R^2$; for a line $ax+by+c=0$, $\varphi = ax+by+c$. The value of
$\varphi$ *is* a cross-track error (zero exactly on the path), and its gradient $\nabla\varphi$ points along
the normal. The guiding vector field is then

$$
\boldsymbol{\chi} \;=\; \underbrace{E\,\nabla\varphi}_{\text{propagate along}} \;-\; \underbrace{k\,\varphi\,\nabla\varphi}_{\text{converge onto}},
\qquad
E = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$E$ is a 90° rotation, so $E\nabla\varphi$ is **tangent** to the level curve (it propagates the robot along
the path) and $-k\,\varphi\,\nabla\varphi$ points down the gradient toward $\varphi = 0$ (it converges). This
is *exactly* the same idea as the closest-point form: $E\nabla\varphi$ plays the role of $\hat{\mathbf{t}}$,
$\nabla\varphi$ the role of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, and $\varphi$ the role of $e$. The level-set view is what
makes the convergence proof clean.

<Callout type="idea" title="Two views, one field.">
Use the **implicit/level-set** form ($\boldsymbol{\chi}=E\nabla\varphi - k\varphi\nabla\varphi$) to reason
about and prove convergence — it needs no closest-point search. Use the **closest-point** form
($\boldsymbol{\chi}=\hat{\mathbf{t}}-k_N e\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$) to actually code it for a Bézier or Hermite
spline, where you don't have a tidy $\varphi$ but you can always project onto the curve.
</Callout>

## Why it provably converges

Take the cross-track error as an energy $V = \tfrac{1}{2}e^2 \ge 0$. Moving along the normalized field, the
rate of change of $e$ is set by the normal component of the velocity, which the converging term makes
proportional to $-e$: $\dot e \propto -k_N\,e$. So

$$
\dot V = e\,\dot e \;\propto\; -k_N\,e^2 \;\le\; 0
$$

The energy only ever **decreases**, and it reaches zero **only** on the path. By Lyapunov's theorem the path
is asymptotically stable: the robot is driven onto the path and *stays* there. A lookahead chasing a moving
point never gets this guarantee — its tracking error depends on speed and lookahead, and it never has a
Lyapunov function in the cross-track error.

In the level-set form the same argument runs on $\varphi$ directly: with $V = \tfrac12\varphi^2$, the
converging term $-k\varphi\nabla\varphi$ gives $\dot\varphi = \nabla\varphi \cdot \dot{\mathbf{p}} \propto
-k\varphi\lVert\nabla\varphi\rVert^2$, so $\dot V \propto -k\varphi^2\lVert\nabla\varphi\rVert^2 \le 0$, and
the propagating term $E\nabla\varphi$ contributes nothing to $\dot\varphi$ because it is orthogonal to
$\nabla\varphi$. The robot slides *along* the path without changing how far off it is, while the converging
term drives that distance to zero.

<Admonition type="warning" title="Where the guarantee bends in practice">

The proof assumes the field is followed exactly and the path's $\varphi$ (or its closest point) is known
everywhere. On a real robot, finite loop time, the closest-point search, actuator limits, and
singular points where $\nabla\varphi = 0$ all chip at it — which is why the
[core lesson](../path-following/guided-vector-fields.mdx) clamps the converge
gain and keeps a minimum along-track speed. The Lyapunov result is the *ideal* the implementation
approximates, not a free lunch.

</Admonition>
