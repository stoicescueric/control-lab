# Calculus

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Calculus studies quantities that change continuously, which makes it the natural language of robot motion.
In FTC, derivatives explain velocity and damping, while integrals explain distance, accumulated error, and energy over time.
</Abstract>

You already use calculus whenever you say "the lift is moving too fast" or "the robot has driven far
enough." The word can sound academic, but the core idea is friendly:

> Calculus is how we talk about a thing changing, and about the total effect of that change.

## FTC example: a lift that overshoots every time

Suppose your lift needs to move from the bottom to a scoring height. If the code jumps instantly from
"go nowhere" to "full target height," the mechanism tries to change position too sharply. The motor
saturates, the lift gains momentum, and by the time the controller reacts, the carriage has already
overshot.

The bug is not just "bad PID." It is a bad motion request. You commanded a position without respecting
velocity and acceleration.

Start with the picture: a robot mechanism has position, velocity, and acceleration at the same time.

## The basics

A **derivative** measures instantaneous rate of change. If $x(t)$ is position, then velocity is:

$$
v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt}
$$

Here, $x(t)$ means position as a function of time, $t$ is time, and $\frac{dx}{dt}$ means "how quickly
position is changing right now."

Acceleration is the derivative of velocity:

$$
a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}
$$

Here, $a(t)$ is acceleration, $v(t)$ is velocity, and $\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$ means "take the derivative of
position twice."

<CalculusIllustration />

An **integral** accumulates a quantity over time. If velocity is known, distance is accumulated velocity:

$$
x(t) = x(0) + \int_0^t v(\tau)\,d\tau
$$

Here, $x(0)$ is the starting position, $\tau$ is a temporary time variable used inside the integral,
and $\int_0^t v(\tau)\,d\tau$ means "add up all the tiny velocity contributions from time $0$ to time
$t$."

On a Control Hub, the loop is discrete. That means calculus becomes finite differences and sums:

$$
v_k \approx \frac{x_k - x_{k-1}}{\Delta t},
\qquad
x_k \approx x_{k-1} + v_k \Delta t
$$

Here, $k$ is the current loop number, $k-1$ is the previous loop, and $\Delta t$ is the measured time
between those two loops.

This is why measuring $\Delta t$ matters. If your loop sometimes takes 8 ms and sometimes 40 ms, the
same encoder change implies a different velocity.

## Engineering use case: motion profiling

Motion profiling is calculus made practical. Instead of commanding a lift to teleport to a target, you
create a path through position, velocity, and acceleration:

$$
\text{target} = \big(x(t), v(t), a(t)\big)
$$

The target is not a single number. It is a package containing desired position, desired velocity, and
desired acceleration at the current instant.

Then feedforward can ask for the voltage needed to produce the target velocity and acceleration, while
feedback cleans up the remaining error:

$$
u = k_S\operatorname{sign}(v) + k_Vv + k_Aa + k_P(x_\text{target} - x_\text{measured})
$$

Here, $u$ is the motor command, $k_S$ fights static friction, $k_V$ scales with velocity, $k_A$ scales
with acceleration, and $k_P$ corrects position error.

That equation explains why a profiled lift feels calm: the controller is no longer surprised by motion.

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: derivatives amplify noise">

Encoder velocity is usually estimated from changes in encoder position. Differentiation magnifies
jitter, so raw velocity can be noisy even when position looks stable. This is why derivative terms and
velocity controllers often need filtering.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="FTC intuition">

When a robot "slams" into a target, ask what acceleration you accidentally demanded. When odometry
drifts, ask what velocity was integrated for too long. Those are calculus questions wearing robot
clothes.

</Admonition>
