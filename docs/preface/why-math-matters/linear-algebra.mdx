# Linear Algebra

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Linear algebra studies vectors, matrices, and transformations, which makes it the language of robot geometry.
In FTC, it explains pose, field-centric driving, mecanum wheel mixing, odometry updates, and the measurement models used by estimators.
</Abstract>

Linear algebra starts with a simple idea: some quantities only make sense as a package. A robot pose is
not just $x$, not just $y$, and not just heading. It is all of them together.

$$
\mathbf{x} =
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y \\
\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

That package is a **vector**. Here, $x$ and $y$ are field position coordinates, and $\theta$ is the
robot's heading angle.

## FTC example: field-centric drive feels rotated

Your driver pushes the joystick away from the alliance wall and expects the robot to move upfield. But
the robot is turned 90 degrees, so robot-forward is now sideways. Without coordinate transforms, the
same joystick command means different things depending on heading.

The driver says, "field-centric is broken." Linear algebra says, "you forgot to rotate a vector."

## Visual intuition

Vectors are arrows; matrices are transformations that move, rotate, stretch, or combine those arrows.

## The basics

A vector stores related components:

$$
\mathbf{v} =
\begin{bmatrix}
v_x \\
v_y
\end{bmatrix}
$$

A matrix maps one vector into another:

$$
\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}
$$

Here, $\mathbf{x}$ is the input vector, $A$ is the matrix doing the transformation, and $\mathbf{y}$ is
the output vector after the transformation.

For a robot, the most important early matrix is rotation. If the robot heading is $\theta$, a field
velocity can be converted into the robot frame by rotating by $-\theta$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
v_x^\text{robot} \\
v_y^\text{robot}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & \sin\theta \\
-\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
v_x^\text{field} \\
v_y^\text{field}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

That is field-centric drive in one equation. Here, $v_x^\text{field}$ and $v_y^\text{field}$ are the
driver's desired field-relative velocity components, while $v_x^\text{robot}$ and $v_y^\text{robot}$
are the commands after converting them into the robot's own forward/strafe frame.

## Engineering use case: FTC mecanum wheel mixing

In FTC, mecanum drive is usually the first place where linear algebra stops feeling optional. A driver
moves two joysticks and a trigger: forward/back, strafe, and turn. The robot has to convert that one
driver intent into four motor commands every loop.

Before the motors are involved, the driver command is a chassis velocity:

$$
\mathbf{u} =
\begin{bmatrix}
v_x \\
v_y \\
\omega
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Here, $\mathbf{u}$ is the chassis command vector, $v_x$ is robot-forward velocity, $v_y$ is sideways
velocity, and $\omega$ is angular velocity, or turn rate.

The result is a wheel-speed vector:

$$
\mathbf{w} =
\begin{bmatrix}
w_{FL} \\
w_{FR} \\
w_{BL} \\
w_{BR}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Here, $\mathbf{w}$ stores the four wheel speeds: front-left, front-right, back-left, and back-right.

For this convention, positive $v_x$ means drive forward, positive $v_y$ means strafe left, and positive
$\omega$ means rotate counterclockwise. The wheel-mixing equation is:

$$
\mathbf{w} =
\frac{1}{r}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 & -(L + W) \\
1 & 1 & (L + W) \\
1 & 1 & -(L + W) \\
1 & -1 & (L + W)
\end{bmatrix}
\mathbf{u}
$$

Here, $r$ is wheel radius. $L$ is the distance from the robot center to the front/back wheel line, and
$W$ is the distance from the robot center to the left/right wheel line. The term $L + W$ describes how
strongly robot rotation contributes to wheel speed. The signs depend on wheel order, roller direction,
and your chosen coordinate convention.

That matrix is exactly the FTC software problem: combine forward, strafe, and turn without writing four
unrelated motor formulas by hand. A clean implementation computes the four wheel speeds, finds the
largest magnitude, and scales all four together if any command is outside the legal motor range:

$$
\mathbf{w}_\text{normalized}
=
\frac{\mathbf{w}}{\max(1, |w_{FL}|, |w_{FR}|, |w_{BL}|, |w_{BR}|)}
$$

Here, $\mathbf{w}_\text{normalized}$ is the motor command vector after desaturation. The denominator is
at least $1$, so small commands are not amplified, but large commands are compressed while preserving
the requested drive direction.

For a visual sanity check, open this Desmos graph: [Basic Mecanum Movement With Odo](https://www.desmos.com/calculator/x39el6adbw).
Use it to connect the abstract vector $\mathbf{u}$ to the way an FTC robot actually translates,
strafes, rotates, and accumulates odometry on the field.

Different teams use different sign conventions depending on wheel order and coordinate choices, but
the engineering idea is stable: wheel mixing is a linear transformation.

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: matrices do not remove saturation">

The matrix may output one wheel speed larger than the motor can physically produce. Production code
must normalize or desaturate the wheel commands while preserving the direction of the requested chassis
motion as much as possible.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="FTC intuition">

If the robot drives diagonally when it should drive straight, check coordinate frames. If one wheel
clips before the others, check the vector after the matrix multiply. Linear algebra gives you places to
look.

</Admonition>
