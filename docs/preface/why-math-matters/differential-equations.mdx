# Differential Equations

<Difficulty level="Medium" />

<Abstract>
Differential equations describe systems by relating a quantity to its own rate of change.
In robotics, they explain why mechanisms do not respond instantly: motors spin up, arms fall under gravity, flywheels coast down, and projectiles slow under drag.
</Abstract>

A differential equation sounds intimidating until you translate the phrase:

> The current state tells you how the state is about to change.

That is exactly how physical mechanisms behave.

## FTC example: the same motor command behaves differently under load

Your intake spins quickly in the air. Then it touches a game element and slows down. The code did not
change, but the mechanism did.

That is not a software mystery. The motor is a dynamic system. Voltage, back-EMF, friction, inertia,
and load determine acceleration, and acceleration changes speed.

## Visual intuition

Differential equations are often easier to understand as arrows showing "where the system wants to go
next." That is why phase-space pictures are so useful.

## The basics

The simplest differential equation says a quantity changes in proportion to the gap between where it is
and where it wants to settle:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = k(x_\infty - x)
$$

Here, $x$ is the current state, $x_\infty$ is the value the system approaches if nothing changes, and
$k$ controls how quickly the system moves toward that value.

<DifferentialEquationsIllustration />

If $x$ is flywheel speed, $x_\infty$ is the speed the motor would eventually reach at the current
voltage, and $k$ controls how quickly it approaches.

A more physical motor model writes angular acceleration as the result of torque minus load:

$$
J\frac{d\omega}{dt} = \tau_\text{motor} - \tau_\text{load}
$$

Here, $J$ is rotational inertia, $\omega$ is angular velocity, $\tau_\text{motor}$ is the torque
produced by the motor, and $\tau_\text{load}$ is the opposing torque from the mechanism or game
element. The motor torque depends on current, and current depends on voltage minus back-EMF:

$$
\tau_\text{motor} = k_t I,
\qquad
I = \frac{V - k_e\omega}{R}
$$

Here, $k_t$ is the motor torque constant, $I$ is current, $V$ is applied voltage, $k_e$ is the back-EMF
constant, and $R$ is motor winding resistance.

Substitute them:

$$
J\frac{d\omega}{dt}
=
k_t\frac{V - k_e\omega}{R}
- \tau_\text{load}
$$

That equation is the motor lesson in dynamic form.

## Engineering use case: flywheel recovery

A flywheel shooter does not only need a target speed. It needs to recover after a game element steals
energy from the wheel. A differential-equation view tells you why recovery time depends on motor
voltage, motor constants, flywheel inertia, contact load, and battery sag.

If the wheel is too light, it loses speed quickly. If it is too heavy, it recovers slowly. The best
design is a tradeoff, and differential equations are how you reason about that tradeoff instead of
guessing.

<Admonition type="warning" title="Hardware reality: models bend near limits">

The equations above are idealized. Real FTC mechanisms add gearbox friction, belt compliance, voltage
sag, current limiting, wheel slip, and sensor delay. The model is still useful because it tells you
which physical effect to look for when behavior changes.

</Admonition>

<Admonition type="tip" title="FTC intuition">

When something "takes time to settle," you are looking at a differential equation. PID tuning changes
the shape of that settling; feedforward changes the destination the system is trying to settle toward.

</Admonition>
