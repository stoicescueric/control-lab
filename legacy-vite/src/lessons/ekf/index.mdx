The Kalman filter is optimal — for *linear* systems. Robot localisation is not linear: range and
bearing, heading, and AprilTag geometry all involve trigonometry. The Extended Kalman Filter is the
adaptation that makes the Kalman machinery work on the curved models real robots actually have, and
it is the most widely deployed nonlinear estimator in existence.

<Problem title="Drift and nonlinear measurements corrupt field coordinates">

Standard wheel-encoder odometry drifts over time — most aggressively during the wheel slip of
high-acceleration turns — steadily corrupting the robot's absolute field coordinates. The natural
fix is to correct it with an absolute sensor, but those measurements are **nonlinear** functions of
the pose: a vision target or beacon reports *range and bearing*, related to $(x, y)$ through
$\sqrt{\cdot}$ and $\operatorname{atan2}$. A plain Kalman filter, which assumes a linear
measurement matrix $H$, cannot fold them in. Worse, the nonlinearity is uneven: a small bearing
error becomes a large position error at long range.

</Problem>

<Theory title="Linearise about the current mean with a Jacobian">

The EKF keeps the Kalman predict–update heartbeat but, at each step, **linearises the nonlinear
motion and measurement models around the current mean estimate** using their *Jacobians* — the
matrices of partial derivatives that give the local slope. A curve, viewed close to your current
estimate, is approximately its tangent line; on that local linearisation the ordinary Kalman update
applies.

For a measurement model $z = h(x)$, the linearisation is the Jacobian
$H = \left.\dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x}\right|_{\hat{x}}$, and the update uses the *true*
nonlinear $h$ for the residual while using $H$ for the covariance:

$$
H = \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}\bigg|_{\hat{x}}, \qquad
\hat{x} = \hat{x}^- + K\,\big(z - h(\hat{x}^-)\big), \qquad
K = P^- H^\top (H P^- H^\top + R)^{-1}
$$

For a radar/beacon at the origin observing position $(x, y)$ via range $r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ and
bearing $\beta=\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)$, the Jacobian is:

$$
H = \begin{bmatrix} x/r & y/r \\[4pt] -y/r^2 & x/r^2 \end{bmatrix}
$$

The $1/r^2$ terms encode exactly the "angle errors matter more at range" effect. The demo tracks a
target from noisy range + bearing; raise the **angle noise** and the far blips fan into wide arcs,
yet the EKF estimate barely moves — it weights each measurement by the geometry the Jacobian
captures.

<EKF />

<Callout type="warn" title="The approximation has limits.">
Linearisation is only valid if the model is gently curved near the estimate. If the nonlinearity is
severe or the estimate is far off, the tangent misleads the filter — which is why the Unscented
Kalman Filter and particle filters exist. For typical robot localisation, the EKF is the workhorse.
</Callout>

</Theory>

<Deploy title="Where this runs on a robot">

This predict → linearise → update loop is precisely what production localisation does when it fuses
drifting odometry with intermittent AprilTag observations: odometry advances the state (predict), a
detected tag provides a nonlinear measurement (update), and the Jacobian weights it by range and
geometry. The matrix algebra is the same `linalg` used in this demo.

<JavaCode league="FTC" label="EKF update structure (per loop, when a tag is seen)">{`// predict: advance pose with odometry, grow covariance P
x = motionModel(x, wheelDeltas);
P = add(mul(mul(F, P), transpose(F)), Q);     // F = motion Jacobian

// update: a tag gives nonlinear measurement z = h(x)
double[][] H  = measurementJacobian(x, tag);  // partials of h at current x
double[][] yk = sub(z, h(x, tag));            // residual uses the true nonlinear h
double[][] S  = add(mul(mul(H, P), transpose(H)), R);
double[][] K  = mul(mul(P, transpose(H)), inv(S));
x = add(x, mul(K, yk));
P = mul(sub(identity(n), mul(K, H)), P);`}</JavaCode>

That closes the **filter** track — turning noisy measurements into a trustworthy state estimate.
The curriculum's other half, *deciding what to do* with that estimate, is the controllers; the whole
stack is then synthesised in the capstone.

</Deploy>
